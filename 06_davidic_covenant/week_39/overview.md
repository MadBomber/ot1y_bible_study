# Week 39: The Kingdom Divided

**Covenant:** [[overview|Davidic Covenant]]
**Week 39** | 1 Kings 12–22

## The Big Picture

Solomon dies, and his son Rehoboam inherits a kingdom that is already cracking. The northern tribes, exhausted by Solomon's building projects and heavy taxation, send a delegation asking for relief. Rehoboam's older advisors counsel moderation. His younger friends counsel severity. Rehoboam chooses the young men's advice — and with a single arrogant speech, he tears the kingdom in half. Ten tribes follow Jeroboam northward. Only Judah and Benjamin remain with Rehoboam in the south. The united monarchy that Saul, David, and Solomon built over a century of blood and prayer disintegrates in an afternoon.

Jeroboam, fearing that his people will return to Rehoboam if they keep going to Jerusalem to worship, commits the sin that will define the northern kingdom for its entire existence: he builds two golden calves, places them at Dan and Bethel, and tells the people, "Behold your gods, O Israel, who brought you up out of the land of Egypt" (1 Kings 12:28). The words are a direct echo of Aaron's golden calf in Exodus 32. Israel's first king repeats Israel's first idolatry. From this point forward, the northern kingdom is judged by a single standard: every king either continues in "the sins of Jeroboam" or he doesn't. Almost all of them do.

Into this spiritual wreckage steps Elijah — the greatest prophet of the Old Testament and the one whose return Malachi will prophesy as the herald of the Messiah (Malachi 4:5). His confrontation with Ahab and the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel is one of the most dramatic scenes in all of Scripture. "How long will you go limping between two different opinions? If the LORD is God, follow him; but if Baal, then follow him" (1 Kings 18:21). Fire falls from heaven. The prophets of Baal are destroyed. Rain returns after three years of drought. And then — in one of the Bible's most psychologically honest moments — Elijah collapses under a broom tree and asks God to let him die. The greatest victory is immediately followed by the deepest depression. But God does not rebuke him. He feeds him. He lets him sleep. He speaks to him — not in the wind, not in the earthquake, not in the fire, but in "a low whisper" (1 Kings 19:12).

## Key Themes

- **The cost of arrogance** — Rehoboam's refusal to listen splits the kingdom God built through David. Leadership that serves itself destroys what leadership that serves others creates. The contrast with David — who danced before the ark, who fed Mephibosheth at his table — is absolute.
- **The repetition of idolatry** — Jeroboam's golden calves echo Exodus 32 with deliberate precision. Israel's besetting sin is not atheism — it is syncretism, the worship of the true God mixed with the worship of false ones. "How long will you go limping between two different opinions?" is a question that remains urgent.
- **Elijah's humanity** — The man who calls down fire from heaven collapses under a bush the next day and wants to die. The Bible does not hide the fragility of its greatest figures. God's response is not a lecture but bread, water, sleep, and a whisper. Depression is met with provision, not rebuke.
- **The still small voice** — God is not in the wind, the earthquake, or the fire. He is in the whisper. The God who thundered at Sinai now speaks in a voice so quiet it requires silence to hear. This is the voice that will one day say, "Peace, be still" — and a storm will obey.

## Christ in This Week

Elijah is the prophet the New Testament most frequently connects to Christ. John the Baptist comes "in the spirit and power of Elijah" (Luke 1:17). At the transfiguration, Elijah appears with Moses, speaking with Jesus about "his departure, which he was about to accomplish at Jerusalem" (Luke 9:31). And Elijah's question on Carmel — "If the LORD is God, follow him" — is the question Jesus poses in a different form: "Who do you say that I am?" (Matthew 16:15). Both demand a decision. Both refuse the luxury of neutrality.
