# Week 40: Elijah and Elisha

**Covenant:** [[overview|Davidic Covenant]]
**Week 40** | 2 Kings 1–13

## Overview

Elijah does not die. A chariot of fire and horses of fire descend, a whirlwind catches him up, and the man who called down fire from heaven ascends in fire to heaven (2 Kings 2:11). Elisha watches, tears his clothes, and picks up Elijah's fallen mantle. He strikes the Jordan — the waters part, just as they did for Moses and Joshua — and the sons of the prophets recognize that "the spirit of Elijah rests on Elisha" (2 Kings 2:15). The prophetic succession is established. The mantle passes. The work continues.

Elisha's ministry is marked by a different texture than Elijah's. Where Elijah was fire and confrontation, Elisha is multiplication and mercy. He purifies poisoned water. He multiplies a widow's oil until every jar is full. He raises a dead boy to life. He feeds a hundred men with twenty loaves — and there are leftovers (2 Kings 4:44). The parallels to Jesus are striking and deliberate: multiplication of food, raising the dead, healing the sick, feeding the many with the little. Elisha is doing on a small scale what Christ will do on a cosmic one.

The healing of Naaman the Syrian (2 Kings 5) is one of the Old Testament's most significant stories for understanding God's plan for the nations. A pagan military commander, afflicted with leprosy, comes to Israel's prophet and is told to wash seven times in the Jordan. Naaman is furious — the rivers of Damascus are cleaner than this muddy stream. But his servants persuade him. He dips. He rises clean. And he declares, "Behold, I know that there is no God in all the earth but in Israel" (2 Kings 5:15). Jesus will later use this story to make a point that nearly gets him killed: "There were many lepers in Israel in the time of the prophet Elisha, and none of them was cleansed, but only Naaman the Syrian" (Luke 4:27). God's grace crosses ethnic boundaries. It always has.

The section climaxes with Elisha's servant trembling before a surrounding army, and Elisha's response: "Do not be afraid, for those who are with us are more than those who are with them" (2 Kings 6:16). God opens the servant's eyes, and the hills are full of horses and chariots of fire. The invisible army of God is always larger than the visible threat.

## Key Themes

- **The mantle passes** — Prophetic ministry continues beyond individual prophets. Elisha receives Elijah's spirit and carries the work forward. The principle applies to every generation: the mission does not depend on one person. It depends on the God who empowers each generation's servants.
- **Miracles of multiplication** — Elisha's miracles consistently involve *more*: more oil, more bread, more life. They are previews of the abundance Christ will bring — five loaves feeding five thousand, water becoming wine, death giving way to resurrection. The God of Israel is not a God of scarcity.
- **Naaman: grace for the outsider** — A pagan enemy commander finds healing in Israel's river. The story demolishes the assumption that God's grace is limited to Israel. Jesus uses it to make exactly that point — and his hometown tries to throw him off a cliff for saying it (Luke 4:28-30).
- **"Those who are with us"** — The invisible reality is always larger than the visible crisis. Elisha's prayer for opened eyes is the prayer of faith in every generation: let me see what is actually true, not merely what appears to be true.

## Christ in This Week

Elisha's miracles are a preview reel of Christ's ministry. Oil multiplied — water turned to wine. A dead boy raised — Jairus's daughter, the widow's son, Lazarus. Twenty loaves feeding a hundred — five loaves feeding five thousand. Naaman washed in the Jordan — the leper who says to Jesus, "Lord, if you will, you can make me clean" (Matthew 8:2). And Elijah's ascent in fire anticipates Christ's ascension: "He was lifted up, and a cloud took him out of their sight" (Acts 1:9). The prophets rehearse. Christ performs.
