# Week 33: David's Anointing and Rise

**Covenant:** [[overview|Davidic Covenant]]
**Week 33** | 1 Samuel 16–20

## The Big Picture

God sends Samuel to Bethlehem — to the house of a man named Jesse — to anoint the next king. Jesse parades his sons before the prophet. The eldest is tall and impressive. Samuel is certain: this must be the one. God corrects him with a sentence that redefines how leadership will be measured for the rest of Scripture: "Do not look on his appearance or on the height of his stature, because I have rejected him. For the LORD sees not as man sees: man looks on the outward appearance, but the LORD looks on the heart" (1 Samuel 16:7). Seven sons pass. None is chosen. Samuel asks if there are others. Jesse hesitates — the youngest is out tending sheep. No one thought to bring him. David arrives ruddy, with beautiful eyes, and the LORD says, "Arise, anoint him, for this is he" (1 Samuel 16:12). The Spirit of the LORD rushes upon David, and from that day forward, the Spirit departs from Saul.

What follows is one of the most famous stories in all of literature. A Philistine giant named Goliath challenges Israel's army for forty days. No one will face him. David — a shepherd boy with no armor, no sword, no military training — volunteers. Saul tries to dress him in royal armor. It doesn't fit. David goes out with a sling and five smooth stones and a declaration of faith so audacious it sounds reckless: "You come to me with a sword and with a spear and with a javelin, but I come to you in the name of the LORD of hosts" (1 Samuel 17:45). The stone strikes. The giant falls. And Israel's future king is introduced to the nation through the most dramatic upset in military history.

David's rise produces two responses: Jonathan's love and Saul's jealousy. Jonathan, Saul's own son and rightful heir, recognizes in David what his father cannot — the anointed one of God — and binds himself to David in a covenant of friendship so deep that it costs him a throne. Saul, consumed by the women's song ("Saul has struck down his thousands, and David his ten thousands"), descends into paranoid rage, hurling spears, setting traps, and hunting the man God has chosen to replace him. The anointed king spends years as a fugitive. The crown is his. The throne is not — not yet.

## Key Themes

- **"The LORD looks on the heart"** — This is the Davidic covenant's thesis statement. God's criteria for leadership are not the world's. Height, appearance, birth order — none of it matters. What matters is the heart's orientation toward God. David is "a man after his own heart" (1 Samuel 13:14) — not because he is sinless but because his heart turns toward God even in failure.
- **The shepherd-king** — David comes to the throne from the pasture. He protects sheep before he leads a nation. Jesus will claim the same identity: "I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep" (John 10:11). The king who fights the giant is the shepherd who fights the wolf.
- **Victory through weakness** — David defeats Goliath not with superior firepower but with inferior equipment wielded in the name of a superior God. This is the logic of the cross: "God chose what is weak in the world to shame the strong" (1 Corinthians 1:27).
- **Jonathan's selfless love** — The rightful heir surrenders his claim to the throne because he recognizes God's anointing on another. Jonathan's love for David is one of the most beautiful relationships in the Old Testament — and a model of what it means to decrease so that another may increase (cf. John 3:30).
- **The anointed fugitive** — David is anointed king but spends years hunted, homeless, and in danger of his life. The gap between anointing and enthronement is the space where faith is forged. Christ, too, is anointed at his baptism and does not take his throne until after the cross.

## Christ in This Week

David is anointed by the Spirit, rejected by the ruling power, and hunted as a fugitive — the pattern Christ will follow precisely. The shepherd boy who faces Goliath alone while the armies of Israel watch from a distance is the image of Christ confronting sin and death on behalf of a people who cannot fight for themselves. And Jonathan — the man who gives up his own rightful inheritance out of love for the anointed one — anticipates John the Baptist: "He must increase, but I must decrease" (John 3:30).
