# Week 36: The Davidic Covenant

**Covenant:** [[overview|Davidic Covenant]]
**Week 36** | 2 Samuel 7, Psalms 2, 110

## Overview

This week steps away from narrative to dwell in the royal psalms — the songs Israel wrote about its king, songs that reach so far beyond any human monarch that they could only be fully understood after the resurrection. These psalms are not wishful thinking. They are prophecy set to music. They describe a king who is simultaneously David's son and David's Lord, a ruler whose dominion extends to the ends of the earth, a priest-king who holds an office that predates Aaron and outlasts every dynasty. The early church recognized immediately that these psalms were about Jesus — and the New Testament quotes them more frequently than almost any other Old Testament texts.

Psalm 2 opens with the nations raging against "the LORD and against his Anointed" — his *Mashiach*, his Messiah. God's response is laughter from heaven, followed by the decree: "You are my Son; today I have begotten you" (Psalm 2:7). This verse is applied to Jesus at his baptism (Matthew 3:17), his transfiguration (Matthew 17:5), and his resurrection (Acts 13:33). The divine sonship of the king is the theological bridge between the Davidic covenant and the incarnation.

Psalm 110 is the most quoted Old Testament passage in the New Testament. "The LORD says to my Lord: 'Sit at my right hand, until I make your enemies your footstool'" (Psalm 110:1). Jesus uses this psalm to stump the Pharisees: if the Messiah is David's son, how can David call him "Lord"? (Matthew 22:41-46). The answer the Pharisees cannot give is the answer the New Testament proclaims: the Messiah is both David's descendant and David's God. Psalm 110 also introduces the Melchizedek priesthood — "You are a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek" (Psalm 110:4) — the priestly order the author of Hebrews will spend three chapters expounding.

Psalm 89 celebrates the Davidic covenant with exuberant confidence — "I will establish your offspring forever and build your throne for all generations" (Psalm 89:4) — and then, in its second half, collapses into anguished lament: "How long, O LORD? Will you hide yourself forever?" (Psalm 89:46). The tension between the promise and the apparent failure of the monarchy is the tension the entire Old Testament lives in. It will not be resolved until an angel speaks to a virgin in Nazareth.

Psalm 72 envisions a king whose reign brings justice to the poor, peace to the nations, and abundance to the earth — "May he have dominion from sea to sea, and from the River to the ends of the earth!" (Psalm 72:8). Psalm 45 celebrates the king's beauty, his truth, and his bride — and Hebrews 1:8-9 applies it directly to Christ: "Your throne, O God, is forever and ever."

## Key Themes

- **Divine sonship** — Psalm 2:7 declares the king as God's "Son." In the ancient Near East, kings were adopted as divine sons. But the New Testament reads this as ontological, not merely ceremonial: Jesus is the Son of God not by adoption but by nature. The decree spoken over Israel's king is the reality lived by Israel's Messiah.
- **Priest-king** — Psalm 110:4 unites two offices Israel kept strictly separate. No king could be a priest. No priest could be a king. But the Messiah will be both — "a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek." This is the office Christ holds: reigning from the throne and interceding from the altar simultaneously.
- **The tension of unfulfilled promise** — Psalm 89's shift from celebration to lament captures the entire experience of Israel between the covenant and the exile. The promise is sure. The evidence is discouraging. Faith lives in the gap.
- **Justice and beauty** — Psalms 72 and 45 paint the Messiah's reign in colors no human monarchy has ever achieved: perfect justice for the poor, peace for the nations, beauty that captivates, and a kingdom that has no end. These are not descriptions of Solomon. They are descriptions of Christ.

## Christ in This Week

The royal psalms are Christ's own self-description. He quotes Psalm 110 to challenge the Pharisees (Matthew 22:44). Peter quotes Psalm 2 at Pentecost (Acts 2:34-35). The author of Hebrews builds his entire Christology around Psalms 2, 45, and 110 in his opening chapter. These are not Old Testament texts that happen to apply to Jesus. They are texts that were *always about* Jesus — written by David, about David's son, who is also David's Lord.
