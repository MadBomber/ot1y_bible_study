# Week 32: Saul: The People's King

**Covenant:** [[overview|Davidic Covenant]]
**Week 32** | 1 Samuel 9–15

## Overview

The people wanted a king like all the nations, and God gives them exactly what they asked for. Saul is everything a king should look like: tall, handsome, from a prominent family, standing "head and shoulders above" everyone else (1 Samuel 9:2). He is Israel's king by appearance — chosen, in a sense, by the same criteria the world always uses. And for a brief, shining moment, it works. The Spirit of God rushes upon Saul. He prophesies among the prophets. He rallies Israel against the Ammonites at Jabesh-gilead and wins a decisive victory. Samuel presents him at Mizpah, and the people shout, "Long live the king!"

But the cracks appear almost immediately. At Gilgal, waiting for Samuel to come and offer the pre-battle sacrifice, Saul grows impatient. The army is deserting. The Philistines are gathering. Samuel is late. So Saul does what no king has the authority to do — he offers the sacrifice himself, seizing priestly prerogative that does not belong to him (1 Samuel 13:9). Samuel arrives and delivers the verdict: "You have done foolishly... your kingdom shall not continue" (1 Samuel 13:13-14). God is already looking for "a man after his own heart."

The final rupture comes in chapter 15. God commands Saul to destroy the Amalekites completely — every person, every animal, everything. Saul obeys partially. He keeps the best livestock and spares King Agag. When confronted by Samuel, Saul offers the excuse that will echo through every generation of religious disobedience: "I have obeyed the voice of the LORD... but the people took of the spoil" (1 Samuel 15:20-21). Samuel's response is one of the most quoted sentences in the Old Testament: "Has the LORD as great delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices, as in obeying the voice of the LORD? Behold, to obey is better than sacrifice" (1 Samuel 15:22).

Saul's failure is not a failure of competence. It is a failure of the heart. He looks right. He sounds right. But he does not submit to God's word when it conflicts with his own judgment. He is the king Israel asked for — the king who looks like a king — and he demonstrates exactly why looking like a king is not the same as being one.

## Key Themes

- **The king who looks right** — Saul is chosen by human criteria: height, appearance, family status. God will later tell Samuel, when choosing Saul's replacement, "The LORD sees not as man sees: man looks on the outward appearance, but the LORD looks on the heart" (1 Samuel 16:7). Saul is the cautionary tale behind that statement.
- **Partial obedience as disobedience** — Saul's sin with the Amalekites is not total rebellion. It is selective compliance — obeying the parts of God's command that suit him and revising the rest. This is the most common form of disobedience in religious life, and Samuel calls it what it is: "rebellion" and "presumption" (1 Samuel 15:23).
- **"To obey is better than sacrifice"** — The sacrificial system itself is not the point. The point is the heart that offers the sacrifice. Ritual without obedience is noise. This principle will run through the prophets (Isaiah 1:11-17; Amos 5:21-24; Micah 6:6-8) and reach its climax in Christ, whose sacrifice is the ultimate act of obedience (Hebrews 10:5-10).
- **The kingdom "shall not continue"** — God rejects Saul's dynasty before it begins. The throne will go to another — "a man after his own heart" (1 Samuel 13:14). The king Israel wanted has failed. The king God wants is tending sheep in Bethlehem.

## Christ in This Week

Saul is the anti-type of Christ — the king who looks right but fails at the heart level. Where Saul seizes priestly authority that does not belong to him, Christ legitimately holds both offices: king and priest. Where Saul offers partial obedience and blames the people, Christ offers total obedience "to the point of death, even death on a cross" (Philippians 2:8). And Samuel's principle — "to obey is better than sacrifice" — finds its ultimate expression in the one whose sacrifice *is* obedience: "When Christ came into the world, he said... 'Behold, I have come to do your will, O God'" (Hebrews 10:5, 7).
