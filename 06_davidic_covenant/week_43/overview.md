# Week 43: The Major Prophets

**Covenant:** [[overview|Davidic Covenant]]
**Week 43** | Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel

## The Big Picture

The prophets are not foretellers of the future. They are forthtellers of the truth — men called by God to stand before kings and nations and declare what is actually happening beneath the surface of events. They diagnose. They warn. They weep. They promise. And woven through every oracle, every vision, every judgment speech, is the thread that makes this study possible: they see someone coming. A servant. A king. A shepherd. A branch. A son of man. The prophets describe Christ from multiple angles, centuries before his birth, with a precision that only divine authorship can explain.

This week offers curated selections from the three major prophets — not comprehensive coverage but the Christological highlights that reveal the coming Messiah most clearly.

**Isaiah** speaks with a dual voice: searing judgment and breathtaking hope, often in the same chapter. His call in chapter 6 — "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts; the whole earth is full of his glory!" (Isaiah 6:3) — establishes the theological baseline: God is holy, and Israel is not. The Immanuel prophecy of chapter 7 and the child-king of chapter 9 ("Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace") point to a ruler no merely human figure could be. And the suffering servant of Isaiah 52:13–53:12 is the Old Testament's most detailed portrait of the cross — written seven centuries before crucifixion was invented: "He was pierced for our transgressions; he was crushed for our iniquities; upon him was the chastisement that brought us peace, and with his wounds we are healed" (Isaiah 53:5).

**Jeremiah** is the weeping prophet — called before birth, rejected by his people, and commissioned to preach a message no one wants to hear: judgment is coming, and it cannot be averted. But embedded in his oracles of destruction is the promise of the new covenant (Jeremiah 31:31-34) — the covenant written on hearts, the covenant the Last Supper will inaugurate.

**Ezekiel** writes from exile, among the deportees by the Kebar canal. His visions are cosmic — wheels within wheels, living creatures, the glory of God departing the temple in stages. But his most Christological passage is the shepherd oracle of chapter 34: "I myself will search for my sheep, and will seek them out... I will set up over them one shepherd, my servant David, and he shall feed them" (Ezekiel 34:11, 23). Jesus will say, "I am the good shepherd" (John 10:11) — and every listener who knew Ezekiel 34 would hear the claim: I am the one the prophet saw.

**Daniel** — though sometimes classified separately — provides the exile's narrative and apocalyptic perspectives. His faithfulness in Babylon (the lions' den, the fiery furnace) demonstrates that God's sovereignty extends beyond Israel's borders. His visions will be explored more fully in the Consummation section.

## Key Themes

- **The holiness of God** — Isaiah 6 is the starting point for all prophetic theology. Until you see God as Isaiah saw him — high, lifted up, surrounded by seraphim crying "Holy!" — you cannot understand why the prophets speak with such urgency. The distance between God's holiness and Israel's sin is the gap the entire sacrificial system was built to address — and the gap only Christ can finally close.
- **The suffering servant** — Isaiah 53 is the Old Testament's most sustained prophecy of the cross. The servant is despised. He is pierced. He is silent before his accusers. He bears the sin of many. He is numbered with the transgressors. He makes intercession for them. Philip will use this very passage to explain the gospel to the Ethiopian eunuch (Acts 8:32-35).
- **The weeping prophet and the new covenant** — Jeremiah's tears are not weakness. They are the proper response to watching a beloved nation destroy itself. But from the deepest grief comes the highest promise: a covenant that transforms from within.
- **The good shepherd** — Ezekiel 34 indicts Israel's leaders as false shepherds who feed themselves instead of the flock. God declares that he will shepherd his people himself — through "one shepherd, my servant David." Jesus claims this identity in John 10 and demonstrates it on the cross.

## Christ in This Week

The prophets do not merely predict Christ. They *describe* him — his character, his mission, his suffering, his reign — with a cumulative precision that no single prophet could achieve alone. Isaiah sees the virgin's son, the suffering servant, the mighty God. Jeremiah sees the new covenant mediator. Ezekiel sees the good shepherd. Daniel sees the sovereign Lord of all nations. Together they paint a portrait so detailed that when Jesus arrives and says, "Today this Scripture has been fulfilled in your hearing" (Luke 4:21), the only question is whether you will believe your own prophets.
