# Week 35: David the King

**Covenant:** [[overview|Davidic Covenant]]
**Week 35** | 2 Samuel 1–10

## The Big Picture

Saul is dead. The path to the throne is open. But David does not celebrate — he mourns. His lament over Saul and Jonathan is one of the most achingly beautiful poems in the Old Testament: "How the mighty have fallen!" (2 Samuel 1:19). David weeps for the man who hunted him. He honors the king who tried to kill him. There is no triumphalism in this ascent — only grief, and then gradual, patient movement toward the crown God promised.

David is first anointed king over Judah at Hebron. A civil war follows — seven years of conflict between David's house and the remnants of Saul's dynasty. Then the elders of all Israel come to David, and he is anointed king over the entire nation. He conquers Jerusalem — the Jebusite stronghold that no tribe had been able to take — and makes it his capital. The "City of David" will become the center of Israel's worship, the site of the temple, and the city where Christ will be crucified and raised.

David's first act as king of united Israel is to bring the ark of the covenant to Jerusalem. The procession is marked by dancing so uninhibited that his wife Michal despises him for it (2 Samuel 6:16). David does not care. He is worshiping the God who kept every promise. The king dances before the ark — and the king's city becomes God's city.

Then comes the night that changes everything. David, resting in his palace, tells Nathan the prophet that he wants to build God a house. God responds with a reversal that is the theological center of the entire Davidic narrative: "The LORD declares to you that the LORD will make *you* a house" (2 Samuel 7:11). David wanted to build a building. God promises to build a dynasty. "Your house and your kingdom shall be made sure forever before me. Your throne shall be established forever" (2 Samuel 7:16). This is the Davidic covenant — unconditional in its ultimate scope, eternal in its reach, and fulfilled in only one descendant: the one the angel will announce to Mary as the one who "will reign over the house of Jacob forever, and of his kingdom there will be no end" (Luke 1:33).

## Key Themes

- **The patient king** — David does not seize the throne. He waits for God to give it — seven years in Hebron as king of only Judah, then the whole nation comes to him. The pattern established during the fugitive years continues: God's timing, not David's ambition.
- **Jerusalem: the city of God** — David's conquest of Jerusalem is not merely strategic. It is theological. The city that will host the temple, the crucifixion, and the resurrection begins here as a Jebusite fortress conquered by Israel's king. Every subsequent reference to Zion traces back to this moment.
- **The covenant reversal** — David offers to build God a house. God offers to build David a house. The reversal captures the entire dynamic of grace: we come to God with our plans, and he replaces them with his own — always greater, always more than we imagined.
- **"Forever"** — The word appears seven times in 2 Samuel 7. The Davidic covenant is not a lease. It is an eternal promise. Every subsequent failure of David's descendants — and there will be many — must be read against this word. The promise outlasts the failure.
- **Mephibosheth** — David seeks out the last surviving member of Saul's family — a crippled man hiding in obscurity — and brings him to eat at the king's table "always" (2 Samuel 9:7). This is grace in narrative form: the enemy's grandson, broken in body, invited to the king's table not because of what he can offer but because of a covenant the king made with his father.

## Christ in This Week

The Davidic covenant of 2 Samuel 7 is the foundation on which the entire New Testament rests. "Your throne shall be established forever" finds its fulfillment in one person and one person only. Peter declares it at Pentecost: "God has made him both Lord and Christ, this Jesus whom you crucified" (Acts 2:36). Paul opens Romans with it: "descended from David according to the flesh and declared to be the Son of God in power according to the Spirit of holiness by his resurrection from the dead" (Romans 1:3-4). And the story of Mephibosheth — the crippled enemy brought to the king's table — is the gospel in miniature. We are all Mephibosheth: broken, hiding, brought to the table by a king who remembers a covenant we did not make.
