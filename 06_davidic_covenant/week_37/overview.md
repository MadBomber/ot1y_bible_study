# Week 37: Sin and Restoration

**Covenant:** [[overview|Davidic Covenant]]
**Week 37** | 2 Samuel 11–24

## Overview

"In the spring of the year, the time when kings go out to battle, David sent Joab... But David remained at Jerusalem" (2 Samuel 11:1). The sentence is devastating in its restraint. The king who should be at war is at home. He is on the roof. He sees a woman bathing. And from a single glance, the man after God's own heart sets in motion a chain of events that will produce adultery, murder, a dead infant, a raped daughter, a murdered son, a civil war, and a kingdom fractured along fault lines that will never fully heal.

The account of David and Bathsheba is the Bible's most unflinching portrait of how sin works in the life of a person who genuinely knows God. David does not plan a crime. He lingers where he should not linger. He sends for what he should not send for. He takes what is not his. And when Bathsheba becomes pregnant, the cover-up is methodical, calculated, and ultimately murderous — Uriah the Hittite, Bathsheba's husband, is positioned in the fiercest battle and abandoned by his comrades. He dies. David marries the widow. "But the thing that David had done displeased the LORD" (2 Samuel 11:27).

Nathan the prophet comes with a parable about a rich man who steals a poor man's only lamb. David erupts with righteous fury: "The man who has done this deserves to die!" Nathan delivers the four most devastating words in the Old Testament: "You are the man" (2 Samuel 12:7). David's response is immediate, unqualified, and raw: "I have sinned against the LORD" (2 Samuel 12:13). Psalm 51 is the prayer he writes afterward — the prayer that every sinner since has borrowed: "Create in me a clean heart, O God, and renew a right spirit within me" (Psalm 51:10).

The consequences that follow are severe and sequential: the child dies. Amnon rapes Tamar. Absalom murders Amnon. Absalom raises a rebellion. David flees Jerusalem, weeping and barefoot over the Mount of Olives — the same route Jesus will walk toward Gethsemane. The kingdom tears itself apart. David is restored to the throne, but the scars never fully heal. The Davidic covenant survives David's worst sin. But the cost of that sin is written across the remaining chapters in blood.

## Key Themes

- **Sin's anatomy** — David's fall follows a precise sequence: idleness, sight, desire, action, cover-up, murder. James 1:14-15 describes the same trajectory: "Each person is tempted when he is lured and enticed by his own desire. Then desire when it has conceived gives birth to sin, and sin when it is fully grown brings forth death."
- **"You are the man"** — Nathan's confrontation is the model of prophetic speech: truth spoken to power, wrapped in a story that disarms before it convicts. David's response — immediate confession without excuse — is the difference between him and Saul. Saul blamed others. David blamed himself.
- **Consequences that survive forgiveness** — Nathan tells David, "The LORD also has put away your sin; you shall not die" (2 Samuel 12:13). But the sword does not depart from David's house. Forgiveness is real and complete. Consequences are also real and lasting. Grace does not erase history.
- **The barefoot king** — David ascending the Mount of Olives, weeping, head covered, barefoot — this image burns itself into Israel's memory and into the reader's imagination. Jesus will ascend the same mountain, in the same grief, toward the same kind of betrayal. The parallel is not accidental.
- **The threshing floor** — David's purchase of Araunah's threshing floor (2 Samuel 24:18-25) to build an altar and stop a plague is the site where Solomon will build the temple (2 Chronicles 3:1) — and where Abraham once raised a knife over Isaac. The geography of redemption is consistent.

## Christ in This Week

David's flight over the Mount of Olives is the Old Testament image behind Jesus' journey to Gethsemane — betrayed by a trusted companion, weeping, ascending the same slope toward the same kind of suffering. But where David fled *from* judgment, Jesus walks *into* it. And Psalm 51 — David's prayer of confession — becomes the prayer of every sinner who stands before the cross: "Against you, you only, have I sinned" (Psalm 51:4). The "clean heart" David begs for is the heart Ezekiel will promise in the new covenant (Ezekiel 36:26) — the heart Christ's death and resurrection make possible.
