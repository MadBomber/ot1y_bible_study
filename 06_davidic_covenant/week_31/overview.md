# Week 31: The Rise of Samuel

**Covenant:** [[overview|Davidic Covenant]]
**Week 31** | 1 Samuel 1–8

## Overview

The book of Judges ended with a sentence that reads like a death certificate: "Everyone did what was right in his own eyes." The book of Samuel opens with a woman weeping in a temple. Hannah is barren — and in the ancient world, barrenness is not a medical condition but a social death sentence. She goes to Shiloh, where the tabernacle sits under the corrupt stewardship of Eli the priest, whose sons treat the sacrifices with contempt and the worshipers with violence. The priesthood is rotting from the inside. The people have no prophet. The word of the LORD is "rare in those days; there was no frequent vision" (1 Samuel 3:1). Israel is at its lowest point since the judges — but God is about to do what he always does. He starts with a prayer.

Hannah's prayer — desperate, silent, so intense that Eli mistakes her for a drunk — produces Samuel, the last judge and the first prophet of the monarchy era. Her song of praise after his birth (1 Samuel 2:1-10) is one of the great theological poems of the Old Testament, celebrating a God who "raises up the poor from the dust" and "lifts the needy from the ash heap." Mary's Magnificat will echo it almost word for word a thousand years later. The God who hears barren women is the God who will overshadow a virgin.

Samuel grows up in the temple, hearing God's voice in the night — "Speak, LORD, for your servant hears" (1 Samuel 3:9) — and becomes the bridge between two eras. Under his leadership, the ark is lost to the Philistines and returned, Israel repents at Mizpah, and the nation experiences a renewal of faithfulness. But Samuel grows old, his sons prove corrupt (the cycle repeats), and the elders come to him with a demand that will change Israel forever: "Appoint for us a king to judge us like all the nations" (1 Samuel 8:5).

God tells Samuel that the people have not rejected him — they have rejected God himself as their king. The request for a human monarch is not merely a political decision. It is a theological one. And yet God grants it — not because it is the best path but because he will work through even Israel's misguided desires to bring about his purposes. The king they are asking for will fail. The king God has in mind will reign forever.

## Key Themes

- **God starts with the barren** — Hannah joins Sarah, Rebekah, and Rachel in the line of barren women through whom God works. The pattern is consistent: human emptiness is God's preferred starting point. The barren womb will reach its climax in the virgin womb.
- **The corruption of the priesthood** — Eli's sons treat the offerings with contempt and the women at the tabernacle as sexual conquests. When the mediators become corrupt, the whole system fails. Israel needs a better priesthood — one that cannot be corrupted by the men who hold it.
- **"Speak, LORD"** — Samuel's response to God's voice in the night is the model of prophetic receptivity. It is the opposite of Babel's "Let us make a name for ourselves." It is the posture Christ himself will take: "Not my will, but yours, be done" (Luke 22:42).
- **Wanting a king "like all the nations"** — Israel's demand exposes their deepest failure: they want to be normal. They want what the nations have. They want a visible, impressive, human ruler instead of the invisible, holy, divine one. God gives them what they ask for — and the consequences will teach them what they truly need.

## Christ in This Week

Hannah's song celebrates a God who reverses the world's power structures — the mighty brought low, the humble exalted, the hungry filled, the barren bearing seven — and ends with a startling prophecy: "The LORD will judge the ends of the earth; he will give strength to his king and exalt the horn of his anointed" (1 Samuel 2:10). The word "anointed" is *mashiach* — Messiah. Hannah speaks of a king before Israel has one. She sees what the elders of chapter 8 cannot: the king Israel needs is not "like all the nations." He is the anointed one of the LORD.
