# New Covenant: The Better Mediator

**Weeks 45–49**

## Overview

In five weeks you will hear the prophetic voices that spoke into the silence after exile — and the ones who, centuries earlier, had already seen beyond the old covenant to something radically new. Jeremiah promised a covenant written on hearts, not stone. Ezekiel envisioned dry bones raised to life by God's Spirit. Joel foresaw that Spirit poured out on all flesh. Meanwhile, the returning exiles — Ezra, Nehemiah, Esther — rebuilt walls and worship, yet the deeper rebuilding these prophets described still awaited its fulfillment at a table in an upper room: "This cup is the new covenant in my blood."

## The Foundation

The old covenant's temporary nature was understood by the prophets. Jeremiah proclaimed God's intention:

> "Behold, the days are coming, declares the LORD, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and the house of Judah, not like the covenant that I made with their fathers… I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts." — Jeremiah 31:31-33

Key distinctions:

- **Old covenant:** inscribed on stone; **New covenant:** written on hearts
- **Old covenant:** multiple priests and sacrifices; **New covenant:** singular priest and sacrifice
- **Old covenant:** exposed sin; **New covenant:** removes sin permanently with promised forgiveness

Ezekiel emphasized spiritual transformation: God replaces a heart of stone with a responsive heart of flesh and indwells believers with His Spirit (Ezekiel 36:26-27). The new arrangement represents fundamentally altered relational dynamics — powered by divine presence rather than human performance.

## In Your Study

In the NT companion study, the Last Supper (Luke 22:20; John 13–16) inaugurates the new covenant in Christ's blood. Hebrews 8–10 makes the sustained argument that Christ mediates a better covenant with better promises. At Pentecost (Acts 2:16-18), Peter declares the Spirit's outpouring as fulfillment of Joel's prophecy. Paul develops new covenant life in 2 Corinthians 3–5.

## Looking Ahead

The new covenant operates presently for all believers, yet awaits complete fulfillment. The Spirit functions as a security deposit for future completion. Upon Christ's return, the deepest covenant promise materializes: "They shall all know me, from the least of them to the greatest" (Jeremiah 31:34) — eliminating need for instruction, struggle, and incomplete understanding. Final communion will occur face-to-face in complete knowledge and restoration.

## The Personal Dimension

The new covenant is the most explicitly personal of all: "I will put my law *within them*, and I will write it on *their hearts*" (Jeremiah 31:33). No longer mediated through stone tablets, priests, or temples — God writes directly on the individual heart. Ezekiel sees God replacing each person's heart of stone with a heart of flesh (Ezekiel 36:26). Joel sees the Spirit poured out on "all flesh" — sons, daughters, old, young, servants. The new covenant fulfills what every previous covenant pointed toward: a God who does not merely command from outside but transforms from within. This is the covenant the thief on the cross entered with nothing but belief and a plea: "Jesus, remember me" (Luke 23:42). And Jesus' response was immediate, personal, and complete: "Today you will be with me in paradise."

> "And I will give you a new heart, and a new spirit I will put within you." — Ezekiel 36:26
