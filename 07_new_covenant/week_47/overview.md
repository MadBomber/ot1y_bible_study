# Week 47: The New Covenant Prophets

**Covenant:** [[overview|New Covenant]]
**Week 47** | Jeremiah 31, Ezekiel 36–37

## Overview

This is the week the Old Testament looks farthest forward — past the exile, past the return, past even the second temple — to a reality so radically new that the prophets can only describe it in the language of resurrection and re-creation. Three prophetic voices converge on the same vision: a covenant that does not merely demand obedience but *produces* it, written not on stone but on the human heart, powered not by human willpower but by the Spirit of God.

**Jeremiah 31** is the pinnacle. Writing as Babylon's armies close in on Jerusalem — with destruction certain and exile inevitable — Jeremiah speaks the most hopeful words in the Old Testament: "Behold, the days are coming, declares the LORD, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and the house of Judah, not like the covenant that I made with their fathers... I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts. And I will be their God, and they shall be my people" (Jeremiah 31:31-33). Every previous covenant operated from outside — commands inscribed on stone, mediated by priests, enforced by consequences. The new covenant will operate from inside. The law will not merely be known. It will be *wanted*. And the final promise is the most staggering: "I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more" (Jeremiah 31:34). Not "I will manage their sin." Not "I will reduce their sin." "I will remember it no more."

**Ezekiel 36** adds the mechanism: "I will give you a new heart, and a new spirit I will put within you. And I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and give you a heart of flesh. And I will put my Spirit within you, and cause you to walk in my statutes" (Ezekiel 36:26-27). This is not reformation. It is resurrection — a dead heart replaced with a living one, the Spirit of God himself powering the obedience the old covenant could only demand.

**Ezekiel 37** makes the resurrection imagery explicit. A valley of dry bones — utterly dead, beyond any human remedy — is reassembled by the word of the prophet and brought to life by the breath (*ruach* — spirit, wind, breath) of God. "I will put my Spirit within you, and you shall live" (Ezekiel 37:14). The vision is about Israel's national restoration, but the New Testament reads it as the image of what God does with every believer: dead in sin, made alive in Christ (Ephesians 2:1-5).

The week closes with Isaiah's servant songs (42, 49) — the mysterious figure who is both Israel and greater than Israel, called to be "a covenant for the people, a light for the nations" (Isaiah 42:6) — and with two promises that reach toward Pentecost: Isaiah 55's invitation ("Come, everyone who thirsts, come to the waters") and Joel 2's explosive prophecy: "I will pour out my Spirit on all flesh" (Joel 2:28).

## Key Themes

- **Internal transformation** — The new covenant's defining feature is not a new set of commands but a new operating system. The law moves from stone to flesh, from outside to inside, from demand to desire. This is the difference between trying harder and being made new.
- **The heart of stone replaced** — Ezekiel's image is surgical. God does not repair the old heart. He removes it and installs a new one. This is not improvement. It is replacement. The theology of regeneration — being "born again" (John 3:3) — begins here.
- **"I will remember their sin no more"** — This is not divine amnesia. It is a legal declaration: the sin will never again be held against the sinner. The author of Hebrews quotes this verse as the culminating argument for the superiority of Christ's sacrifice: "Where there is forgiveness of these, there is no longer any offering for sin" (Hebrews 10:18). The sacrificial system is over.
- **The Spirit poured out** — Joel's promise — not on priests only, not on prophets only, but on "all flesh" — is the democratization of the divine presence. At Pentecost, Peter will stand up and declare: "This is what was uttered through the prophet Joel" (Acts 2:16). The fire that once burned on Sinai now rests on the head of every believer.
- **The servant as covenant** — Isaiah does not say the servant *mediates* a covenant or *brings* a covenant. He says the servant *is* the covenant: "I will give you as a covenant to the people" (Isaiah 49:8). Christ does not merely deliver the new covenant. He embodies it. He is the promise in person.

## Christ in This Week

Every passage this week points directly to Christ and the cross. The new covenant of Jeremiah 31 is inaugurated at the Last Supper: "This cup that is poured out for you is the new covenant in my blood" (Luke 22:20). The new heart of Ezekiel 36 is what Christ provides through the Spirit he sends after the resurrection. The dry bones of Ezekiel 37 are what Paul describes when he writes, "You were dead in the trespasses and sins... But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us, even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ" (Ephesians 2:1, 4-5). The servant of Isaiah 42 and 49 is the one who will say in a synagogue in Nazareth, "Today this Scripture has been fulfilled in your hearing" (Luke 4:21). And Joel's Spirit is the Spirit Christ breathes on his disciples: "Receive the Holy Spirit" (John 20:22).
