# Week 49: Between the Testaments

**Covenant:** [[overview|New Covenant]]
**Week 49** | Daniel 9, Isaiah 42, 49, 55

## Overview

This is the final week before the Old Testament's eschatological finale — a week devoted to the passages that bridge the testaments with the greatest specificity and the deepest pathos. These texts do not merely predict the Messiah. They describe his mission, his suffering, his rejection, and his vindication with a detail that reads less like prophecy and more like eyewitness testimony written in advance.

**Daniel 9** records a prayer of repentance that becomes the occasion for the most precise chronological prophecy in the Old Testament. The angel Gabriel appears and announces "seventy weeks" — seventy sets of seven — "decreed about your people and your holy city, to finish the transgression, to put an end to sin, and to atone for iniquity, to bring in everlasting righteousness, to seal both vision and prophet, and to anoint a most holy place" (Daniel 9:24). The prophecy speaks of an "anointed one" who will be "cut off and shall have nothing" (Daniel 9:26) — a Messiah who arrives, accomplishes atonement, and is killed. Whatever interpretive framework one brings to the seventy weeks, the core claim is unmistakable: the Messiah's death is the means by which transgression is finished, sin is ended, and everlasting righteousness is established.

**Daniel 10–12** expands the vision into the sweep of history — empires rising and falling, angels warring behind the scenes, and a final vision of resurrection: "Many of those who sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt" (Daniel 12:2). This is the Old Testament's clearest statement of bodily resurrection — the doctrine that will explode into history on Easter morning.

**Isaiah's servant songs** (42, 49, 50) trace the mission of a figure who is both Israel and greater than Israel — called from the womb, equipped with God's Spirit, commissioned to bring justice to the nations, and yet "despised and rejected by men" (Isaiah 53:3). The fourth song — Isaiah 52:13–53:12 — is the most extraordinary passage in the Old Testament. It describes a man who is disfigured beyond recognition, who bears the sins of others, who is led like a lamb to slaughter, who is silent before his accusers, who is buried with the rich, and who — after his suffering — "shall see his offspring; he shall prolong his days" (Isaiah 53:10). Written seven centuries before Calvary, it reads like a medical report from the cross.

The week closes with two psalms that Jesus himself quoted in his final hours. **Psalm 22** — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — describes pierced hands and feet, garments divided by lot, and bones out of joint. **Psalm 16** — "You will not abandon my soul to Sheol, or let your holy one see corruption" — is the psalm Peter quotes at Pentecost to explain the resurrection (Acts 2:25-28). The Old Testament ends not in silence but in anticipation — every thread drawn taut, every promise straining toward fulfillment, the entire story leaning forward into the moment when the silence breaks and the Word becomes flesh.

## Key Themes

- **The anointed one "cut off"** — Daniel 9:26 is the Old Testament's most direct statement that the Messiah will die. Not in old age, not in battle, but "cut off" — executed, terminated, having accomplished the atonement described in verse 24. The cross is not Plan B. It was planned before the foundation of the world.
- **Resurrection** — Daniel 12:2 breaks the Old Testament's relative silence on the afterlife with a statement of stunning clarity. The dead will rise. Some to life. Some to judgment. Jesus quotes this passage (John 5:28-29), and Paul builds the doctrine of resurrection on the same foundation (1 Corinthians 15).
- **The servant who suffers** — Isaiah 53 is the passage the Ethiopian eunuch is reading when Philip encounters him (Acts 8:32-35). "About whom does the prophet say this, about himself or about someone else?" Philip tells him the good news about Jesus. The answer has always been Jesus.
- **Psalm 22 — the cross in advance** — "They have pierced my hands and my feet" (Psalm 22:16). "They divide my garments among them, and for my clothing they cast lots" (Psalm 22:18). These are not metaphors. They are descriptions of crucifixion — written a thousand years before Rome invented the practice.
- **Psalm 16 — the resurrection in advance** — "You will not abandon my soul to Sheol, or let your holy one see corruption" (Psalm 16:10). Peter's argument at Pentecost is simple: David died and was buried, and his tomb is still here. But the psalm cannot be about David. It must be about David's greater son — the one whose body did not see corruption because God raised him on the third day (Acts 2:29-32).

## Christ in This Week

This week is the Old Testament's final close-up of the coming Messiah — and every detail is confirmed by the New Testament. Daniel's anointed one, cut off to make atonement. Isaiah's servant, pierced for our transgressions, crushed for our iniquities, silent before his accusers, bearing the sin of many. The psalmist's cry of dereliction — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — and the psalmist's confidence in resurrection — "You will not let your holy one see corruption." These are not scattered predictions. They are a portrait, painted over centuries by different hands, of a single person. When Jesus opens the Scriptures to the two disciples on the road to Emmaus and shows them "in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself" (Luke 24:27), these are the Scriptures he means.
