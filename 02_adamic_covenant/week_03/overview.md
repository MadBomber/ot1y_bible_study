# Week 3: The Fall

**Covenant:** [[overview|Adamic Covenant]]
**Week 3** | Genesis 3

## Overview

Everything changes in Genesis 3. The world that was declared "very good" two chapters ago is about to fracture — and the fracture will run through every relationship, every institution, every human heart for the rest of the biblical narrative. But the way it happens matters as much as the fact that it happens. The serpent does not begin with a command or a threat. He begins with a question: "Did God actually say?" (Genesis 3:1). It is the first theological argument in Scripture, and every false theology since has been a variation of it — not a denial of God's existence but a subtle distortion of his word, his character, his goodness. The woman sees, takes, eats, gives. The man, standing beside her, says nothing and eats. And immediately everything changes: eyes opened, nakedness exposed, a frantic scramble for fig leaves, and then — hiding from the God who made them.

But the chapter does not end in darkness. It ends with God walking into the garden and asking a question of his own: "Where are you?" (Genesis 3:9). This is not the question of a deity who has lost track of his creatures. It is the question of a Father who will not let his children go without pursuing them. He confronts. He curses the serpent. He pronounces consequences that are severe and real — pain in childbirth, thorns in the ground, death as the final horizon. And then, buried in the curse on the serpent, he speaks the sentence that sets the rest of the Bible in motion: "I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and her offspring; he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel" (Genesis 3:15). The church fathers called it the *protoevangelium* — the first gospel. A descendant of the woman will destroy the destroyer. The promise is made before a single sacrifice is offered, before a single law is given, before a single covenant is named. Grace precedes everything.

The week closes with two passages that reach forward from the fall toward its resolution. Isaiah 7:14 and 9:6-7 announce that the seed will arrive as a child — "born of a virgin," carrying the government on his shoulders, bearing names that no human being could rightly claim: Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace. And Psalm 51, David's great confession after his adultery with Bathsheba, reveals what the fall looks like a thousand years later in the heart of an individual — and what God's response to genuine repentance always is.

## Key Themes

- **The anatomy of temptation** — The serpent's method is not brute force but subtle distortion: questioning God's word, denying the consequences, making the forbidden look desirable. "The woman saw that the tree was good for food, and that it was a delight to the eyes, and that the tree was to be desired to make one wise" (Genesis 3:6). The pattern — desire of the flesh, desire of the eyes, pride of life — will appear again in 1 John 2:16 and in Jesus' own temptation in the wilderness.
- **God's pursuit of the fallen** — The first move after the fall is not human repentance but divine pursuit. "Where are you?" establishes the pattern for the entire Bible: God seeks before we seek him. He clothes the naked before they ask to be clothed. The first blood in Scripture is shed not by a sinner but by God himself, to cover shame.
- **The protoevangelium** — Genesis 3:15 is the seed from which the entire gospel grows. The promise of a crushing victory through a wounded victor sets the trajectory that leads through every covenant to the cross and the empty tomb.
- **The entrance of death** — "For dust you are, and to dust you shall return" (Genesis 3:19). Death enters as a consequence, not a design feature. Everything that follows in Scripture — the sacrificial system, the prophets' longing, the resurrection — is God's response to this intruder.

## Christ in This Week

The one who will be tempted in the wilderness is the "last Adam" (1 Corinthians 15:45) — and where the first Adam fell, the last Adam will stand. The serpent's question "Did God actually say?" will be answered by a man quoting Deuteronomy in a desert: "It is written." The seed promised in Genesis 3:15 is the same child announced in Isaiah 9:6 — "For to us a child is born, to us a son is given." And the God who clothed Adam and Eve with animal skins at the cost of blood is the same God who will clothe believers in the righteousness of Christ at the cost of a cross. The first gospel is not a different gospel. It is the same one.
