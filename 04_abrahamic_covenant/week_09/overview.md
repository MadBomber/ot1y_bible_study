# Week 9: The Call of Abram

**Covenant:** [[overview|Abrahamic Covenant]]
**Week 9** | Genesis 12–14

## The Big Picture

After eleven chapters of universal history — creation, fall, flood, Babel — the camera suddenly, dramatically narrows to a single individual. "Now the LORD said to Abram, 'Go from your country and your kindred and your father's house to the land that I will show you'" (Genesis 12:1). No explanation of why Abram. No résumé of qualifications. Joshua 24:2 will later reveal that Abram's family "served other gods." God simply speaks into the life of a pagan man in Mesopotamia and issues a command with a promise attached — a promise so extravagant it will take the rest of the Bible to unfold: "I will make of you a great nation, and I will bless you and make your name great, so that you will be a blessing... and in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" (Genesis 12:2-3).

This is the turning point of the primeval history. Everything before it describes the problem. Everything after it describes the solution. The seventy nations of Genesis 10 are not abandoned. They are the object of the promise. Abram is not chosen instead of the nations but *for* them.

But the man who receives this breathtaking promise is immediately revealed as flawed, fearful, and capable of spectacular failure. Within verses of his arrival in Canaan, a famine drives him to Egypt, where he lies about his wife to save his own skin (Genesis 12:10-20). The father of the faithful begins his journey with a cowardly deception. This is not an accident of narrative — it is a deliberate revelation: the covenant rests on God's faithfulness, not Abram's.

Genesis 13 records Abram's generous separation from Lot, choosing the less desirable land while God reaffirms the promise: "All the land that you see I will give to you and to your offspring forever" (Genesis 13:15). And Genesis 14 introduces the mysterious Melchizedek, king of Salem and "priest of God Most High," who blesses Abram after his military rescue of Lot. The author of Hebrews will spend an entire chapter arguing that this enigmatic priest-king foreshadows Christ — a priesthood not descended from Aaron, not based on genealogy, but eternal (Hebrews 7:1-10).

## Key Themes

- **Election by grace** — God's choice of Abram is unexplained by merit. The call is pure initiative — God reaching into a pagan world and choosing a man for reasons known only to himself. This is the pattern for every calling that follows.
- **Promise before performance** — The blessing of Genesis 12:2-3 is given before Abram does anything. He has not yet believed, obeyed, or been circumcised. The promise creates the faith, not the other way around.
- **Faith and failure coexisting** — Abram's lie in Egypt comes immediately after his obedient departure from Haran. The Bible does not sanitize its heroes. The man of faith is also a man of fear, and God's promise survives both.
- **Blessing for the nations** — "In you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" is the mission statement for the rest of Scripture. Abram is not chosen instead of the nations but as the instrument through whom salvation will reach them.
- **Melchizedek: priest and king** — This figure appears without genealogy, blesses Abram, and receives a tithe. Hebrews reads him as a type of Christ — "a priest forever, after the order of Melchizedek" (Hebrews 7:17), holding both offices that Israel would later separate.

## Christ in This Week

The call of Abram is the beginning of the road to Bethlehem. Matthew opens his Gospel: "The book of the genealogy of Jesus Christ, the son of David, the son of Abraham" (Matthew 1:1). Paul argues that the promise "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" was the gospel preached in advance (Galatians 3:8). And Melchizedek — king of righteousness, king of peace, priest of God Most High — is the first person in Scripture to hold both offices simultaneously. Only one other will: Jesus of Nazareth, "a priest forever, after the order of Melchizedek" (Hebrews 7:17).
