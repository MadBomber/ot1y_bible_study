# Week 10: Covenant of Promise

**Covenant:** [[overview|Abrahamic Covenant]]
**Week 10** | Genesis 15–17

## Overview

This is the week the covenant deepens from promise to oath, from word to ceremony, from declaration to blood. Abram, childless and aging, asks God how the promise can possibly be fulfilled when he has no heir. God takes him outside, points at the sky, and says, "Look toward heaven, and number the stars, if you are able to number them... So shall your offspring be" (Genesis 15:5). And then comes the sentence that Paul will call the foundation of the gospel: "And he believed the LORD, and he counted it to him as righteousness" (Genesis 15:6). Not works. Not circumcision. Not law-keeping. Belief — counted as righteousness.

But God does not stop at a declaration. He formalizes the promise in a covenant ceremony so ancient and so solemn that modern readers almost miss its significance. Abram cuts animals in half and arranges the pieces in two rows. In the ancient Near East, both parties would walk between the pieces, invoking the animals' fate upon themselves if they broke the agreement. But as darkness falls and Abram sinks into a deep sleep, only God passes through — appearing as "a smoking fire pot and a flaming torch" (Genesis 15:17). This covenant is unilateral. God binds himself. God takes the consequences upon himself. Abram sleeps. Centuries later, on a cross outside Jerusalem, God will pay the price he swore between those divided animals.

Genesis 16 reveals what happens when faith falters — Sarai gives Hagar to Abram, Ishmael is born, and the consequences of this detour will echo through centuries. Then Genesis 17 arrives: God appears to Abram at ninety-nine, changes his name to Abraham, introduces circumcision, and promises that Sarah — ninety-year-old Sarah — will bear a son. Abraham falls on his face and laughs. God names the boy Isaac — "he laughs." The God of the covenant has a sense of timing and, it appears, a sense of humor.

## Key Themes

- **Belief counted as righteousness** — Genesis 15:6 is arguably the most important verse in the Old Testament for understanding the gospel. Abraham is declared righteous not on the basis of what he has done but on the basis of what he has believed. Paul builds the entire argument of Romans 4 and Galatians 3 on this single sentence.
- **The unilateral covenant** — God alone walks between the pieces. This is not a contract. It is an oath — sworn by God, guaranteed by God, fulfilled by God. The cross is the payment God swore to make if the covenant was broken. And it was broken. And he paid.
- **Human scheming and divine patience** — Hagar and Ishmael represent the perennial temptation to help God keep his promises. But God does not abandon Hagar — he sees her, names her son, and provides for them. Grace extends even to the consequences of faithlessness.
- **The sign in the flesh** — Circumcision marks every male as belonging to the covenant community. Paul will later argue that the true circumcision is "a matter of the heart, by the Spirit, not by the letter" (Romans 2:29).
- **God's impossible timing** — Abraham is ninety-nine. Sarah is ninety. The son of promise is biologically impossible. That is precisely the point. The God of the covenant specializes in the impossible.

## Christ in This Week

The covenant ceremony of Genesis 15 is the theological foundation for the cross. God swore an oath between divided animals, taking upon himself the penalty for covenant-breaking. When Christ is broken on Calvary — his body torn, his blood poured out — he is paying the debt God swore to pay in the darkness of that ancient night. And Isaac, the son born against all biological possibility to parents who laughed at the promise, is the first of many children in Scripture whose miraculous birth points toward the ultimate miraculous birth — the virgin's son in Bethlehem.
