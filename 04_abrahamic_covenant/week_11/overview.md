# Week 11: Testing and Judgment

**Covenant:** [[overview|Abrahamic Covenant]]
**Week 11** | Genesis 18–20

## Overview

Three visitors arrive at Abraham's tent by the oaks of Mamre, and what begins as an act of ancient Near Eastern hospitality becomes one of the most extraordinary theophanies in the Old Testament. The LORD himself is among the visitors. He reaffirms the promise of a son to Sarah, who laughs behind the tent flap — and is caught: "Is anything too hard for the LORD?" (Genesis 18:14). The question echoes forward through the entire Bible, from the barren wombs of the matriarchs to the virgin birth itself.

Then the narrative darkens. God reveals to Abraham his intention to judge Sodom and Gomorrah, and what follows is the most audacious prayer in Scripture. Abraham bargains with God — "Will you indeed sweep away the righteous with the wicked?" (Genesis 18:23) — negotiating downward from fifty righteous to ten. The prayer reveals something essential about the God of the covenant: he welcomes intercession. He allows himself to be questioned. He does not punish Abraham for pressing the point. And yet the intercession cannot save Sodom. Lot and his family are dragged out by angels as fire falls. The cities are obliterated. Lot's wife looks back and becomes a pillar of salt — and Jesus will later use her as a one-sentence warning about the cost of divided loyalty: "Remember Lot's wife" (Luke 17:32).

The week closes with Genesis 20, where Abraham — astonishingly — repeats the exact failure of Genesis 12. He lies about Sarah again, this time to Abimelech of Gerar. The man who just interceded for an entire city cannot trust God to protect his own wife. The juxtaposition is deliberate and devastating: faith and failure, courage and cowardice, inhabiting the same person on the same journey. This is not a contradiction. It is reality. And the covenant survives it.

## Key Themes

- **"Is anything too hard for the LORD?"** — The question Sarah's laughter provokes is the question the entire Bible answers. From barren wombs to parted seas to empty tombs, the answer is always the same: nothing.
- **Intercession and its limits** — Abraham's prayer for Sodom reveals a God who welcomes bold intercession but is not manipulated by it. God's justice is not negotiable, but his patience is real. The absence of ten righteous people in Sodom is not God's failure but humanity's.
- **Judgment as revelation** — The destruction of Sodom is not divine cruelty. It is the unveiling of what sin produces when given enough time and enough permission. Jesus, Peter, and Jude all use Sodom as a picture of what final judgment looks like (Luke 17:28-30; 2 Peter 2:6; Jude 7).
- **The persistence of failure** — Abraham's repeated lie about Sarah is the Bible's refusal to turn its heroes into saints. The same man who believes the impossible promise cannot bring himself to trust God in an ordinary crisis. Grace does not require perfection. It carries imperfection.

## Christ in This Week

The LORD who appears at Abraham's tent is the pre-incarnate Christ — one of the clearest Old Testament theophanies. The question "Is anything too hard for the LORD?" will be answered definitively at the empty tomb. Abraham's intercession for Sodom foreshadows Christ's intercession for the world — but where Abraham's prayer was limited by the absence of the righteous, Christ's intercession succeeds because *he* is the righteous one who stands in the gap. And the fire that fell on Sodom anticipates the fire of final judgment — but also the fire of Pentecost, where judgment is transformed into salvation.
