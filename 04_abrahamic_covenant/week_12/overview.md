# Week 12: The Son of Promise

**Covenant:** [[overview|Abrahamic Covenant]]
**Week 12** | Genesis 21–24

## The Big Picture

Isaac is born. After twenty-five years of waiting — twenty-five years of wandering, lying, scheming, and clinging to a promise that biology said was impossible — Sarah holds her son and laughs. This time the laughter is not doubt but joy: "God has made laughter for me; everyone who hears will laugh over me" (Genesis 21:6). The impossible has happened. The God who said it would be so has made it so.

But the arrival of the son of promise immediately creates conflict. Ishmael, the son of the flesh, mocks Isaac. Sarah demands that Hagar and Ishmael be sent away. God tells Abraham to listen to Sarah — "for through Isaac shall your offspring be named" (Genesis 21:12) — but he also promises to make Ishmael into a nation. The God of the covenant does not discard those who fall outside the chosen line. He provides for them. But the line of promise runs through Isaac and Isaac alone.

Then comes Genesis 22 — the most harrowing chapter in the Old Testament. "God tested Abraham" (Genesis 22:1). Take your son. Your only son. Isaac. The one you love. Go to Mount Moriah. Offer him as a burnt offering. The commands are stacked with agonizing precision, each one twisting the knife deeper. Abraham rises early, saddles his donkey, takes the wood and the fire and the knife, and goes. For three days he walks toward the mountain, knowing what he has been asked to do. Isaac carries the wood on his back — the wood of his own sacrifice. He asks the question that splits the reader's heart: "Behold, the fire and the wood, but where is the lamb?" (Genesis 22:7). Abraham answers with a sentence that is either desperate faith or unconscious prophecy: "God will provide for himself the lamb for a burnt offering, my son" (Genesis 22:8).

The knife is raised. The angel speaks. The ram appears in the thicket. And Abraham names the place "The LORD will provide" — on the mountain where, centuries later, Solomon will build the temple and where, centuries after that, a cross will stand. The author of Hebrews reads the scene and sees resurrection: Abraham "considered that God was able even to raise him from the dead, from which, figuratively speaking, he did receive him back" (Hebrews 11:19).

The week closes with Sarah's death and burial — Abraham's first piece of owned land in Canaan is a grave — and the beginning of the search for Isaac's bride, a story of providential guidance that will bring Rebekah from Mesopotamia to continue the line.

## Key Themes

- **The God who provides** — *Yahweh-Yireh*, "the LORD will provide," is the name Abraham gives to the mountain. It is not a statement about the past alone but a declaration about the character of God for all time. On that same mountain, God will provide a temple. And on a hill nearby, he will provide a Lamb.
- **Testing, not tempting** — God does not tempt (James 1:13), but he tests. The test of Genesis 22 reveals what Abraham truly loves most. It is the same question every covenant bearer faces: will you hold God's gifts with open hands, or will you grip them so tightly that they become idols?
- **Isaac as a type of Christ** — The parallels are deliberate and devastating: the beloved only son, the father's willingness, the wood carried on the son's back, the three-day journey (Abraham considered Isaac as good as dead for three days), the substitutionary ram, the son received back "as if from the dead."
- **The first piece of the Promised Land** — Abraham buys a cave to bury Sarah. The man who was promised "all the land" (Genesis 13:15) owns exactly one plot — and it contains a grave. The promise is not yet fulfilled. But it will be.

## Christ in This Week

Genesis 22 is the Old Testament's most sustained Christological narrative. A father offers his only beloved son on a mountain. The son carries the wood of his own sacrifice. A substitutionary animal dies in his place. The place is named "The LORD will provide." And the son is received back from the dead. Every element points to Calvary with a precision that the original participants could not have fully understood. When Jesus says, "Abraham rejoiced that he would see my day. He saw it and was glad" (John 8:56), Genesis 22 is the day he means.
