# Week 26: Wandering and Rebellion

**Covenant:** [[overview|Mosaic Covenant]]
**Week 26** | Numbers 15–36

## The Big Picture

The forty years of wandering are mostly silence. The Bible passes over them with a swiftness that is itself a judgment — the generation that refused to enter the land is not given a narrative. They are given a desert. But the episodes that *are* recorded form a relentless pattern: rebellion, judgment, mercy, rebellion again. Korah leads a coup against Moses' authority and the earth swallows him. The people grumble about the aftermath, and a plague kills 14,700. Aaron's rod buds — God's chosen mediator confirmed by a staff that sprouts almonds overnight.

Then comes the moment that costs Moses the Promised Land. At Meribah, God tells Moses to *speak* to the rock and water will flow. Moses, exhausted and furious, strikes the rock twice instead. Water gushes out. The people drink. But God says to Moses and Aaron: "Because you did not believe in me, to uphold me as holy in the eyes of the people of Israel, therefore you shall not bring this assembly into the land" (Numbers 20:12). The severity is startling — and it is a revelation. Even Moses is not above the covenant's demands. Even the mediator must trust the God he serves. Paul will later note that the rock Moses struck the first time (Exodus 17) was Christ (1 Corinthians 10:4). The rock was meant to be struck once. The second striking disrupts the typology of a sacrifice that need not be repeated.

The Balaam narrative (Numbers 22–24) is one of the strangest and most beautiful sequences in the Old Testament. A pagan prophet, hired to curse Israel, finds himself unable to speak anything but blessing. Three times Balak positions Balaam to curse, and three times God puts prophecy on pagan lips. The fourth oracle contains the most explicitly messianic prophecy in the Pentateuch: "A star shall come out of Jacob, and a scepter shall rise out of Israel" (Numbers 24:17). The magi who follow a star to Bethlehem centuries later may be following this very prophecy to its fulfillment.

## Key Themes

- **Authority challenged and confirmed** — Korah's rebellion is not merely political. It is an assault on God's appointed order. The earth's response — swallowing the rebels — is God's answer to the question of who speaks for him. The pattern is sobering: those who reject God's mediators reject God himself.
- **Moses' failure at the rock** — The greatest man in the Old Testament does not enter the Promised Land because of a moment of anger and unbelief. The lesson is not cruelty but clarity: no human mediator is sufficient. The one who will lead God's people into the true rest must be greater than Moses (Hebrews 3:3).
- **"God is not man, that he should lie"** — Balaam's declaration in Numbers 23:19 is one of the clearest statements of God's faithfulness in the Old Testament. What God has spoken, he will do. What he has promised, he will fulfill. No curse can overturn it. No pagan sorcerer can undo it. No human failure can cancel it.
- **The bronze serpent** — When poisonous serpents plague the camp, God tells Moses to make a bronze serpent and lift it on a pole. "Everyone who is bitten, when he sees it, shall live" (Numbers 21:8). Jesus claims this image directly: "As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whoever believes in him may have eternal life" (John 3:14-15).

## Christ in This Week

The bronze serpent is Christ's own chosen metaphor for the cross — an image of the curse lifted up so that those who look upon it in faith are healed. The rock struck at Meribah is Christ struck once for sin (1 Corinthians 10:4) — a sacrifice not to be repeated. Balaam's star and scepter prophecy (Numbers 24:17) points to the king the magi will seek. And Moses' exclusion from the land because of his failure at the rock is the Old Testament's clearest demonstration of why a better mediator is needed — one who does not merely speak for God but *is* God.
