# Week 24: Holy Living

**Covenant:** [[overview|Mosaic Covenant]]
**Week 24** | Leviticus 17–27

## Overview

At the heart of Leviticus — literally at its center chapter — stands the Day of Atonement. One day a year. One priest. One sacrifice for all the sins of all the people. Leviticus 16 describes the most important ritual in Israel's calendar: the high priest enters the Most Holy Place — the only person who ever does, the only day it ever happens — carrying blood that is not his own, to sprinkle on the mercy seat and make atonement for the nation. Then a second goat is brought — the scapegoat, *azazel* — and Aaron lays both hands on its head, confessing "all the iniquities of the people of Israel" (Leviticus 16:21). The goat is sent into the wilderness, carrying the sin away. One goat dies. One goat disappears. Both are needed to picture what atonement means: the penalty paid *and* the sin removed. The author of Hebrews will argue that Christ accomplishes both in a single act (Hebrews 9:11-14; 10:1-18).

From the Day of Atonement, Leviticus moves into what scholars call the Holiness Code (chapters 17–26). The commanding phrase is God's own self-reference: "You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy" (Leviticus 19:2). Holiness is not a human achievement. It is a divine attribute shared with a people who belong to him. The laws that follow cover everything from sexual ethics to business practices, from treatment of the poor to the rhythm of the agricultural calendar. The Year of Jubilee (Leviticus 25) — when debts are forgiven, slaves freed, and land returned to its original owners — is the most radical economic legislation in the ancient world, and Jesus will open his public ministry by quoting from the Jubilee tradition: "The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, because he has anointed me to proclaim good news to the poor... to proclaim the year of the Lord's favor" (Luke 4:18-19).

The book closes with blessings and curses (Leviticus 26) that make the covenant's stakes painfully clear, and a chapter on vows and dedications (Leviticus 27) that reminds Israel that everything — land, animals, people — ultimately belongs to God.

## Key Themes

- **The Day of Atonement** — Yom Kippur is the theological climax of the sacrificial system. Everything in Leviticus 1–15 builds toward this day. The high priest's entrance into the Most Holy Place — alone, with blood, once a year — is the clearest Old Testament picture of what Christ does when he "entered once for all into the holy places... by means of his own blood" (Hebrews 9:12).
- **The scapegoat** — Two goats, one purpose. The first dies — the penalty paid. The second is sent away — the sin removed. Christ is both: "The Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world" (John 1:29). He bears the penalty *and* removes the sin.
- **"Be holy"** — Holiness in Leviticus is not mystical withdrawal from the world. It is practical, embodied, daily. It shapes how you eat, how you do business, how you treat the immigrant, how you harvest your field. Jesus summarizes this entire section with one sentence: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself" (Matthew 22:39; cf. Leviticus 19:18).
- **Jubilee** — Every fiftieth year: debts forgiven, slaves freed, land returned. This is God's vision for economic justice — a regular, systemic reset that prevents permanent poverty and permanent wealth. Jesus' announcement of his ministry in Luke 4 is a Jubilee proclamation. The gospel is Jubilee for the soul.

## Christ in This Week

The Day of Atonement is fulfilled in Christ with devastating completeness. He is the high priest who enters the true holy of holies — heaven itself (Hebrews 9:24). He carries not the blood of goats but his own blood. He does not need to return each year because his sacrifice is final: "He has appeared once for all at the end of the ages to put away sin by the sacrifice of himself" (Hebrews 9:26). And the scapegoat — the sin-bearer sent into the wilderness — is the same image Isaiah uses for the suffering servant: "The LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all" (Isaiah 53:6). The Day of Atonement is the Old Testament's most detailed rehearsal for Good Friday.
