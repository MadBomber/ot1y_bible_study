# Week 23: Holiness and Sacrifice

**Covenant:** [[overview|Mosaic Covenant]]
**Week 23** | Leviticus 1–16

## Overview

Leviticus is the book most Christians skip and the book most essential for understanding the cross. The tabernacle has been built. The glory has filled it. And now God speaks from inside it — not from a mountain at a distance but from a tent in the camp. The entire book of Leviticus is God's answer to a single question: how does a holy God live among an unholy people without destroying them?

The answer is blood. The opening chapters detail five types of offerings — burnt, grain, peace, sin, and guilt — each addressing a different dimension of the broken relationship between God and humanity. The burnt offering is total consecration: the entire animal consumed on the altar, ascending as smoke, a "pleasing aroma to the LORD" (Leviticus 1:9). The sin offering addresses unintentional transgressions. The guilt offering addresses specific violations requiring restitution. Together they form a comprehensive system for dealing with the reality that a holy God cannot coexist with unaddressed sin.

The most critical element is the laying on of hands. "He shall lay his hand on the head of the burnt offering, and it shall be accepted for him to make atonement for him" (Leviticus 1:4). This is not a symbolic gesture. It is a transfer — the worshiper's sin placed on the animal, the animal's death accepted in the worshiper's place. This is substitutionary atonement in its most primitive and powerful form. Every sacrifice in Leviticus is a rehearsal for Calvary. Every lamb on every altar is a shadow of the Lamb who will come.

The consecration of Aaron and his sons (Leviticus 8–9) establishes the priesthood — men set apart by blood, water, and oil to stand between God and the people. Then Leviticus 10 delivers a shock: Nadab and Abihu, Aaron's sons, offer "unauthorized fire before the LORD" and are consumed. The holiness that dwells in the tabernacle is not tame. It is not safe. It requires exactness because it reflects the character of the God who inhabits it.

The purity laws of Leviticus 11–15 — clean and unclean animals, skin diseases, bodily discharges — can seem alien to modern readers, but their purpose is to make holiness tangible in daily life. Every meal, every bodily function, every interaction with disease or death becomes an occasion to remember: you belong to a holy God, and the boundary between clean and unclean matters.

## Key Themes

- **Substitutionary atonement** — The hand on the head of the animal is the theological center of the sacrificial system. Sin is transferred. Death is accepted. The worshiper goes free. "Without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness of sins" (Hebrews 9:22).
- **The cost of holiness** — Nadab and Abihu's death is a terrifying reminder that the God of grace is also the God of consuming fire. Holiness is not flexible. The same God who forgives sin also judges presumption. The cross holds both truths together.
- **"A pleasing aroma"** — The repeated phrase describes the burnt offering's smoke ascending to God. Paul uses the same language for Christ: "Christ loved us and gave himself up for us, a fragrant offering and sacrifice to God" (Ephesians 5:2). The vocabulary of Leviticus is the vocabulary of the cross.
- **Priesthood as mediation** — The priests do not earn God's favor. They channel it. They stand in the gap, handling blood and fire on behalf of a people who cannot approach God directly. Christ is the priest who ends the system by fulfilling it — "he holds his priesthood permanently, because he continues forever" (Hebrews 7:24).

## Christ in This Week

Every sacrifice in Leviticus is a shadow. Christ is the substance. The burnt offering — total consecration — is Christ's complete obedience "to the point of death, even death on a cross" (Philippians 2:8). The sin offering is Christ "who knew no sin" made "to be sin" on our behalf (2 Corinthians 5:21). The laying on of hands is the transfer that Isaiah describes: "The LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all" (Isaiah 53:6). And the blood that the priests carry into the holy place is the blood Christ carries into heaven itself: "He entered once for all into the holy places, not by means of the blood of goats and calves but by means of his own blood, thus securing an eternal redemption" (Hebrews 9:12).
