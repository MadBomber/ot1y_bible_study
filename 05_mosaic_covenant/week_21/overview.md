# Week 21: The Tabernacle

**Covenant:** [[overview|Mosaic Covenant]]
**Week 21** | Exodus 25–31

## Overview

God has delivered his people from Egypt, led them through the sea, fed them with manna, and given them his law. Now he does something even more astonishing: he moves in. "Let them make me a sanctuary, that I may dwell in their midst" (Exodus 25:8). The God who flung galaxies into space and shook Sinai with his voice now asks for a tent. Not a palace. Not a fortress. A portable, collapsible tent made of acacia wood, linen, and animal skins — designed so it can be carried through the wilderness by a nomadic people. The infinite God condescends to inhabit a space roughly forty-five feet long.

The level of detail in these chapters can feel overwhelming to modern readers, but every element is intentional. The ark of the covenant, overlaid with gold and topped by two cherubim, is the throne of God — the mercy seat where atonement is made and God's presence dwells. The table of showbread, holding twelve loaves representing the twelve tribes, declares that God feeds his people in his presence. The golden lampstand (*menorah*) provides the only light in the holy place — God's light in God's house. The altar of incense fills the space with fragrant smoke that rises like prayer. The bronze altar in the courtyard is where blood is shed — the unavoidable cost of approaching a holy God with unholy hands.

And behind the innermost curtain — the veil — sits the Most Holy Place, where only the high priest enters, once a year, on the Day of Atonement, with blood that is not his own. This veil is the barrier between a holy God and a sinful people. It will hang for fifteen centuries until a man hangs on a cross and "the curtain of the temple was torn in two, from top to bottom" (Matthew 27:51). From top to bottom — not by human hands, but by God's. The barrier is removed. The way is open. The tabernacle has done its work.

## Key Themes

- **"That I may dwell in their midst"** — The tabernacle is the answer to the deepest question of the Bible: how can a holy God live among an unholy people? Not by lowering his standards. Not by pretending sin doesn't matter. But by providing a system — a mediating structure of sacrifice, priesthood, and sacred space — that makes it possible.
- **Every detail points to Christ** — The ark is the throne of grace (Hebrews 4:16). The showbread is the bread of life (John 6:35). The lampstand is the light of the world (John 8:12). The altar of incense is the prayers of the saints (Revelation 5:8). The bronze altar is the cross. The veil is Christ's flesh (Hebrews 10:20). The tabernacle is not furniture. It is theology.
- **The priesthood** — Aaron and his sons are consecrated with blood, washed with water, and clothed in garments of "glory and beauty" (Exodus 28:2). They stand between God and the people, offering sacrifices that the people cannot offer for themselves. Christ is the priest who needs no successor because he lives forever (Hebrews 7:24).
- **"Tabernacled" among us** — John 1:14 says the Word became flesh and *eskenosen* — "pitched his tent" — among us. The Greek word deliberately echoes the tabernacle. Jesus' body is the true *mishkan*: the place where God's glory dwells among his people. The tabernacle in the wilderness was always pointing to a body.

## Christ in This Week

The tabernacle is a portrait of Christ painted in wood, gold, linen, and blood. The author of Hebrews makes the connection explicit: the earthly tabernacle is "a copy and shadow of the heavenly things" (Hebrews 8:5). Christ enters "the greater and more perfect tent (not made with hands)" (Hebrews 9:11) with his own blood, securing "an eternal redemption." When the veil tore at the crucifixion, the tabernacle's message was finally delivered in full: the way into God's presence is open, and the one who opened it is both the priest who enters and the sacrifice he carries.
