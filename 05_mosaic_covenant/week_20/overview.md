# Week 20: The Mountain of God

**Covenant:** [[overview|Mosaic Covenant]]
**Week 20** | Exodus 19–24

## Overview

Three months after walking through the sea, Israel arrives at the foot of Mount Sinai — and the ground shakes. Thunder. Lightning. A thick cloud. The sound of a trumpet growing louder and louder. The mountain burns with fire while smoke rises "like the smoke of a kiln" (Exodus 19:18). Boundaries are set. No one may touch the mountain. Even the livestock must be kept back. The holiness of God is not a metaphor at Sinai. It is a physical force that can kill.

But before the thunder, before the commandments, before the fire — God speaks a sentence that reframes everything: "You yourselves have seen what I did to the Egyptians, and how I bore you on eagles' wings and brought you to myself. Now therefore, if you will indeed obey my voice and keep my covenant, you shall be my treasured possession among all peoples, for all the earth is mine; and you shall be to me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exodus 19:4-6). The law that follows is not a ladder to climb. It is the shape of gratitude. God delivered them *first*. Obedience is the response, not the condition. "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt" (Exodus 20:2) — the Ten Commandments begin not with a demand but with a declaration of grace.

The commandments themselves are devastating in their scope. The first four address the relationship between humanity and God: no other gods, no idols, no misuse of the name, remember the Sabbath. The last six address relationships between people: honor parents, do not murder, do not commit adultery, do not steal, do not bear false witness, do not covet. Together they describe the character of the community God is building — a people set apart, shaped by holiness, oriented toward both worship and justice.

The week closes with the Book of the Covenant (Exodus 20:22–23:19), which applies the commandments to specific situations — slaves, violence, property, justice for the poor — and then with the covenant ratification ceremony of Exodus 24, where Moses reads the law, the people respond "All that the LORD has spoken we will do" (Exodus 24:7), and blood is thrown against the altar and on the people. The covenant is sealed in blood. It always is.

## Key Themes

- **Grace before law** — The Ten Commandments do not begin with "You shall." They begin with "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out." The law is given to a people already redeemed, not to a people trying to earn redemption. This sequence — salvation first, obedience second — is the structure of the entire Bible.
- **The holiness that burns** — Sinai reveals that God's holiness is not a gentle quality. It is a consuming fire (Hebrews 12:29). Boundaries must be set. The unprepared cannot approach. This is why the tabernacle, the priesthood, and the sacrificial system will be necessary — and why, ultimately, a mediator greater than Moses will be required.
- **The scope of the law** — The commandments address both worship and ethics, both the vertical relationship with God and the horizontal relationships with neighbors. Jesus will summarize the entire law with two commands that mirror this structure: love God, love neighbor (Matthew 22:37-40).
- **"A kingdom of priests"** — Israel's calling is not merely national but vocational. They are to be a nation of mediators — standing between God and the world, representing God to the nations and the nations to God. Peter applies this identity directly to the church (1 Peter 2:9).
- **Covenant ratified in blood** — Exodus 24's ceremony — blood thrown on the altar and on the people — establishes the pattern that every covenant requires blood. The author of Hebrews will conclude: "Without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness of sins" (Hebrews 9:22).

## Christ in This Week

Jesus stands on another mountain — the Mount of Beatitudes — and delivers another law, reinterpreting Sinai with an authority that surpasses Moses: "You have heard that it was said... but I say to you" (Matthew 5:21-22). No prophet ever spoke this way. Moses said, "Thus says the LORD." Jesus says, "I say to you" — because he is the LORD who spoke at Sinai. The blood of the Mosaic covenant, thrown on the altar and the people, anticipates the blood of the new covenant — "This cup that is poured out for you is the new covenant in my blood" (Luke 22:20). And the calling to be "a kingdom of priests" finds its ultimate expression in Christ, the great high priest who makes his people a "royal priesthood" (1 Peter 2:9).
