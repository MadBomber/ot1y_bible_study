# Week 27: The Farewell of Moses

**Covenant:** [[overview|Mosaic Covenant]]
**Week 27** | Deuteronomy

## The Big Picture

Moses stands on the plains of Moab, looking across the Jordan at a land he will never enter. Behind him: forty years of wilderness, a generation buried in the desert, a lifetime of mediating between a holy God and an ungrateful people. Before him: the next generation, born in the wilderness, raised on manna, about to inherit what their parents refused. Deuteronomy is Moses' farewell sermon — or rather, three sermons — preached on the edge of the Promised Land to a people who must now go forward without him.

The book is structured as a covenant renewal. Moses retells the story (chapters 1–4), restates the law (chapters 5–26), and then lays out the blessings and curses that will follow obedience or disobedience (chapters 27–30). But Deuteronomy is not merely a legal document. It is the most passionate, personal, and emotionally urgent book in the Pentateuch. Moses *pleads*. He reminds. He warns. He begs the people to remember what they have seen — and to choose life.

At its theological heart stands the *Shema*: "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one. You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your might" (Deuteronomy 6:4-5). This is not one commandment among many. It is the commandment — the one Jesus will call "the great and first commandment" (Matthew 22:38). Everything else in the law is commentary on this sentence. Love God. Completely. That is the whole of it.

Moses also delivers the prophecy that will define Israel's messianic expectation: "The LORD your God will raise up for you a prophet like me from among you, from your brothers — it is to him you shall listen" (Deuteronomy 18:15). The people who stood at Sinai and begged for a mediator — "You speak to us, and we will listen; but do not let God speak to us, lest we die" (Exodus 20:19) — will receive one. But the prophet like Moses will not merely relay God's words. He will *be* the Word.

The book ends with Moses ascending Mount Nebo, viewing the land from Pisgah's summit, and dying alone with God. "And there has not arisen a prophet since in Israel like Moses, whom the LORD knew face to face" (Deuteronomy 34:10). The Old Testament's greatest figure exits the stage. The promise continues. The land awaits. The prophet like Moses has not yet come.

## Key Themes

- **The Shema** — "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one." This declaration of monotheism and total allegiance is the heartbeat of Jewish faith and the commandment Jesus elevates above all others. It is not merely theological information. It is a call to love — with everything you are and everything you have.
- **Remember** — The word *zakar* appears throughout Deuteronomy like a drumbeat. Remember Egypt. Remember the wilderness. Remember the manna. Remember Sinai. The greatest danger is not that Israel will face new enemies but that they will forget the God who has already saved them. Spiritual amnesia is the root of every apostasy.
- **Choose life** — "I have set before you life and death, blessing and curse. Therefore choose life" (Deuteronomy 30:19). Moses frames the entire covenant as a choice — not a fate, not a predetermined outcome, but a decision each person and each generation must make.
- **The prophet like Moses** — Deuteronomy 18:15 creates the expectation that a future deliverer will arise who is like Moses but greater. Peter identifies Jesus as this prophet at Pentecost (Acts 3:22). Stephen does the same before the Sanhedrin (Acts 7:37). Moses is the template. Christ is the fulfillment.
- **Moses' death** — The greatest figure in the Old Testament does not enter the Promised Land. He sees it. He cannot reach it. The lesson is not punishment but incompleteness: Moses can bring the people to the border. Only Joshua — whose name, *Yehoshua*, is the Hebrew form of Jesus — can bring them in.

## Christ in This Week

Jesus quotes Deuteronomy more than any other book. In his wilderness temptation, all three of his responses to Satan come from Deuteronomy (8:3, 6:16, 6:13). When asked to name the greatest commandment, he quotes the Shema (Matthew 22:37). And the prophet like Moses — the mediator greater than Moses, the one whom the LORD knows "face to face" — is Christ himself. What Moses could not accomplish — bringing the people into rest — Jesus does. "For if Joshua had given them rest, God would not have spoken of another day later on. So then, there remains a Sabbath rest for the people of God" (Hebrews 4:8-9). The name says it all: Joshua. Jesus. The same name. The same mission. The greater fulfillment.
