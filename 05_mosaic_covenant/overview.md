# Mosaic Covenant: Law, Lamb, and Tabernacle

**Weeks 17–30**

## Overview

In fourteen weeks you will follow Israel from slavery to the edge of the Promised Land — and beyond into conquest and the turbulent era of the judges. This is the largest section of the Old Testament and the richest soil for Christological typology. The Passover lamb, the sacrificial system, the tabernacle, the Day of Atonement, manna in the wilderness, water from the rock — each element foreshadows the one who would fulfill them all. At Sinai God constitutes Israel as "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation," giving the law that Paul would later call "a guardian until Christ came." From Exodus through Ruth, the pattern repeats: God delivers, the people fail, God provides again.

## The Foundation

God liberated Israel from Egyptian slavery through Moses and the ten plagues, leading them to Mount Sinai. There, the covenant established Israel as "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation." The Mosaic covenant encompasses moral, ceremonial, and civil law.

Three foundational elements foreshadow Christ:

1. **Passover lamb** — Unblemished sacrificial animal whose blood protected Israel
2. **Sacrificial system** — Ongoing temple offerings acknowledging sin's requirement for atonement
3. **Tabernacle** — God's dwelling place among the people, mediating the divine-human encounter

Paul describes the law as a "guardian until Christ came," revealing holiness standards and humanity's shortfall.

## In Your Study

- **Last Supper** — Luke 22:1-38 and John 13–16 reveal Christ as the true Passover lamb
- **Crucifixion** — Luke 23 and John 18–19 fulfill the sacrificial system
- **Christ's priesthood and sacrifice** — Hebrews 8–12 demonstrates Christ's superiority over the Levitical priesthood, tabernacle, and sacrifices
- **Law and grace** — Galatians 3–5 develops the law's role as guardian until Christ came

## Looking Ahead

The temporary tent tabernacle evolved into the temple, ultimately finding permanent fulfillment in Revelation's vision. The new Jerusalem requires no physical temple because "its temple is the Lord God the Almighty and the Lamb." God's covenant promise of dwelling among people culminates in Christ and eternal community.
