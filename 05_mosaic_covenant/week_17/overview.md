# Week 17: Slavery and Deliverance

**Covenant:** [[overview|Mosaic Covenant]]
**Week 17** | Exodus 1–4

## The Big Picture

Genesis ended with a coffin in Egypt and a promise that God would visit his people. Four hundred years pass in the space between Genesis 50 and Exodus 1 — and when the curtain rises, everything has changed. The seventy souls who came to Egypt as honored guests are now a slave labor force, and a new Pharaoh "who did not know Joseph" (Exodus 1:8) has turned the descendants of Abraham into bricks-and-mortar chattel. The more they are oppressed, the more they multiply — a detail the text presents as the fulfillment of the Abrahamic promise operating under impossible conditions. God's purposes do not require favorable circumstances. They require only God.

Into this darkness, a child is born, placed in a basket of reeds on the Nile — the same Hebrew word, *tevah*, used for Noah's ark. The parallel is deliberate: once again, God preserves life through a vessel on the water. Moses is drawn out by Pharaoh's daughter, raised in the palace of the oppressor, exiled to the wilderness after killing an Egyptian, and then — at eighty years old, tending sheep in Midian — encounters God in a bush that burns without being consumed.

The burning bush is one of the most important theophanies in Scripture. God reveals his name — "I AM WHO I AM" (*ehyeh asher ehyeh*) — a name that refuses to be contained, controlled, or fully comprehended. It is the name Jesus will claim for himself seven times in John's Gospel: "I am the bread of life," "I am the light of the world," "before Abraham was, I am" (John 8:58). The God who speaks from the bush is the God who will speak from the cross. And the mission he gives Moses — "I have surely seen the affliction of my people... and I have come down to deliver them" (Exodus 3:7-8) — is the mission statement for the incarnation itself.

## Key Themes

- **God hears the cry** — "God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant with Abraham" (Exodus 2:24). The four-hundred-year silence was not divine absence. It was divine patience. The God who "remembered" Noah now "remembers" his covenant — and remembering means acting.
- **The basket and the ark** — The word *tevah* connects Moses' basket to Noah's ark. Both are instruments of salvation through water, both are designed by God's providence, and both preserve the life through whom God's purposes will advance.
- **"I AM"** — God's self-revelation at the bush is the theological center of the Old Testament. The name is not a title but an assertion of absolute, self-existent being. Every "I am" statement Jesus makes in John's Gospel is a claim to this identity.
- **The reluctant deliverer** — Moses offers five objections to God's call (Exodus 3:11, 3:13, 4:1, 4:10, 4:13). God answers every one. The deliverer of Israel is not chosen for his eloquence or courage but for God's purposes. The pattern will repeat with Gideon, Jeremiah, and ultimately with a virgin in Nazareth who says, "How will this be?" (Luke 1:34).

## Christ in This Week

Moses is the Old Testament's primary type of Christ — the deliverer sent by God to rescue his people from bondage. Both are threatened with death as infants. Both are hidden in Egypt. Both emerge from obscurity to confront the powers of the world. And the name God reveals at the bush — "I AM" — is the name Jesus claims as his own, provoking the same response from the religious establishment that Pharaoh will give to Moses: refusal, rage, and an escalation of violence that will end at a cross instead of a sea.
