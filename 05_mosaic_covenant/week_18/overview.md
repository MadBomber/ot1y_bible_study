# Week 18: The Plagues and Passover

**Covenant:** [[overview|Mosaic Covenant]]
**Week 18** | Exodus 5–12

## Overview

"Let my people go." The demand is simple. Pharaoh's refusal is absolute. And what follows is the most sustained confrontation between God and the powers of this world anywhere in the Old Testament — ten plagues that systematically dismantle Egypt's gods, its economy, its confidence, and finally its future. The Nile turns to blood (striking at Hapi, the river god). Frogs swarm the land (Heqet, the frog goddess of fertility). Darkness covers Egypt for three days (Ra, the sun god, rendered impotent). Each plague is not merely a natural disaster. It is a theological argument: "On all the gods of Egypt I will execute judgments. I am the LORD" (Exodus 12:12).

But the plagues are building toward something. The tenth and final blow — the death of every firstborn in Egypt — is the one that will break Pharaoh and birth a nation. And before it falls, God does something unprecedented: he institutes a meal. The Passover lamb must be unblemished, a year old, slaughtered at twilight. Its blood must be smeared on the doorposts and lintel of every Israelite home. The family eats the lamb with unleavened bread and bitter herbs, dressed for travel, sandals on their feet, staff in hand. When the destroyer passes through Egypt at midnight, he will see the blood and pass over the house. The blood does not make the household worthy. It makes them covered.

This is the night that will define Israel forever. Every subsequent generation will reenact this meal, retell this story, and taste this bread. And fifteen centuries later, on the night before he is crucified, a man will take this same bread and say, "This is my body." He will lift this same cup and say, "This cup that is poured out for you is the new covenant in my blood" (Luke 22:19-20). Paul will later write with breathtaking economy: "Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed" (1 Corinthians 5:7). The lamb of Exodus 12 has a name. It has always had a name.

## Key Themes

- **Judgment on the gods of Egypt** — The plagues are not random. They are targeted demolitions of Egypt's religious system. The God of Israel is not one deity among many. He is the God before whom all other claims to divinity collapse.
- **The hardening of Pharaoh's heart** — The text says both that Pharaoh hardened his own heart and that God hardened it. The theological tension is real and is not resolved by choosing one over the other. Human responsibility and divine sovereignty operate simultaneously — the same pattern that will explain the cross (Acts 2:23).
- **The Passover lamb** — Unblemished. A year old. Slaughtered at twilight. Blood on the doorposts. This is the Bible's most detailed foreshadowing of Christ's sacrifice. John the Baptist's declaration — "Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world" (John 1:29) — reaches all the way back to this night.
- **Salvation by blood, not merit** — The blood on the doorpost does not indicate that the household inside is sinless. It indicates that a substitute has died in their place. This is substitutionary atonement in its earliest, most visceral form.
- **The meal that remembers** — The Passover is not merely commemorated. It is reenacted. "You shall observe this rite as a statute for you and for your sons forever" (Exodus 12:24). Jesus transforms this meal into the Lord's Supper — the old feast pointing forward now fulfilled in the new.

## Christ in This Week

The Passover lamb is Christ. The connection is not metaphorical — it is typological, deliberate, and confirmed by the New Testament at every level. The lamb is unblemished (1 Peter 1:19). Its bones are not broken (John 19:36; cf. Exodus 12:46). Its blood is the means of deliverance (Hebrews 9:22). And the timing is precise: Jesus is crucified on Passover afternoon, at the hour when the lambs are being slaughtered in the temple. The God who designed the Passover fifteen centuries earlier designed it around the death of his Son.
