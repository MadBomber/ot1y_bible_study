# Week 29: Conquest and Settlement

**Covenant:** [[overview|Mosaic Covenant]]
**Week 29** | Joshua 13–24

## The Big Picture

The conquest is won but not complete. Joshua is old. Much of the land remains to be taken. And now God commands something that will take longer than a battle and require more faith than a siege: settle in. Live in the land. Divide it among the tribes. Inhabit the promise.

The land distribution of Joshua 13–19 can read like a surveyor's report, but its theological purpose is profound. God is keeping his promise — the promise spoken to Abraham five centuries earlier that his offspring would inherit this land. Every tribal boundary, every city name, every allocation is a line drawn on a map that says: *God keeps his word.* The specificity is the point. This is not a vague spiritual inheritance. It is dirt, rivers, hills, and cities — tangible, measurable, real.

Caleb's request in Joshua 14 is one of the great moments of faith in the Old Testament. At eighty-five years old, the only other surviving spy from the Kadesh-barnea disaster asks Joshua for the hardest territory — the hill country where the giants live: "Give me this hill country of which the LORD spoke on that day... It may be that the LORD will be with me, and I shall drive them out" (Joshua 14:12). The man who forty-five years ago said "We are well able" has not changed his mind.

The cities of refuge (Joshua 20) establish a system of justice — places where those who kill accidentally can flee and find protection until their case is heard. The Levitical cities (Joshua 21) distribute the priestly tribe throughout Israel, ensuring that God's word and God's worship are accessible to every tribe. And Joshua 21:45 delivers the theological summary: "Not one word of all the good promises that the LORD had made to the house of Israel had failed; all came to pass."

Joshua's farewell in chapters 23–24 echoes Moses' farewell in Deuteronomy. He rehearses God's faithfulness, warns against idolatry, and sets the choice before the people with unforgettable clarity: "Choose this day whom you will serve, whether the gods your fathers served in the region beyond the River, or the gods of the Amorites in whose land you dwell. But as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD" (Joshua 24:15). The people swear allegiance. Joshua sets up a stone witness. And then he dies. The question that hangs over the next book is whether the allegiance will hold.

## Key Themes

- **Promise kept in detail** — The land distribution is not bureaucracy. It is theology. Every boundary line is proof that God does what he says. The God who promised Abraham "all the land that you see" (Genesis 13:15) is now parceling it out, tribe by tribe.
- **Caleb's enduring faith** — Forty-five years of wandering did not diminish Caleb's faith. He asks for the hardest assignment, not the easiest. This is what faith looks like when it has been tested by decades: not weaker but sharper, not cautious but hungry for the next challenge.
- **Cities of refuge** — These cities embody a principle that the gospel will fulfill completely: there is a place to run when you are guilty, a shelter from the consequences of what you have done. Christ is the ultimate city of refuge — the one to whom the guilty flee and find safety (Hebrews 6:18).
- **"Choose this day"** — Joshua's challenge strips the veneer of neutrality. There is no middle ground. You serve the LORD or you serve something else. Every day is a choosing day.

## Christ in This Week

The land that Joshua distributes is a foretaste of the inheritance Christ secures for his people — "an inheritance that is imperishable, undefiled, and unfading, kept in heaven for you" (1 Peter 1:4). The cities of refuge point to Christ as the shelter for the guilty. And Joshua's challenge — "choose this day" — is the same challenge the gospel presents to every hearer: "Behold, now is the favorable time; behold, now is the day of salvation" (2 Corinthians 6:2).
