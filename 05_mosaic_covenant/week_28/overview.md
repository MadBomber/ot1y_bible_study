# Week 28: Crossing the Jordan

**Covenant:** [[overview|Mosaic Covenant]]
**Week 28** | Joshua 1–12

## The Big Picture

Moses is dead. The greatest figure in Israel's history is buried in an unmarked grave on the wrong side of the Jordan. And now God speaks to Joshua — and his first word is not comfort but command: "Moses my servant is dead. Now therefore arise, go over this Jordan" (Joshua 1:2). The time for mourning is over. The promise that has been traveling since Genesis 12 — "To your offspring I will give this land" — is about to be kept.

Joshua's name is itself a sermon. *Yehoshua* — "the LORD saves." It is the same name that will be given to a baby in Bethlehem: "You shall call his name Jesus, for he will save his people from their sins" (Matthew 1:21). The one who leads Israel into the Promised Land carries the same name as the one who will lead God's people into eternal rest.

The crossing of the Jordan deliberately echoes the crossing of the Red Sea. The priests carry the ark into the river. The waters stop flowing. The people cross on dry ground. Twelve stones are set up as a memorial. The message is unmistakable: the God who delivered the previous generation from Egypt is the same God delivering this generation into Canaan. His faithfulness does not expire with the people who doubt it.

Jericho falls — not to military strategy but to worship. Israel marches around the city for seven days, the priests blow trumpets, the people shout, and the walls collapse. The first victory in the Promised Land is won by obedience, not by force. But immediately after Jericho comes Ai, where Achan's hidden sin leads to defeat and death. The juxtaposition is deliberate: victory comes through obedience to God; defeat comes through disobedience, even when the disobedience is hidden. The God who topples walls also searches hearts.

Rahab — a Canaanite prostitute who hides the Israelite spies — is the most unlikely hero of the conquest narrative. She confesses faith in a God she has only heard about: "The LORD your God, he is God in the heavens above and on the earth beneath" (Joshua 2:11). She and her family are saved. And she enters the genealogy of Christ (Matthew 1:5). The line of promise runs through a pagan woman who believed what Israel's own generation of spies refused to believe.

## Key Themes

- **Joshua as a type of Christ** — The name, the mission, the role: Joshua leads God's people into the rest that Moses could not provide. The author of Hebrews makes the connection explicit (Hebrews 4:8). What Joshua accomplished partially and temporarily, Jesus accomplishes completely and eternally.
- **The Jordan as baptism** — The crossing echoes the Red Sea, and both echo baptism. The old life on one side, the new on the other. Twelve stones mark the crossing, just as twelve apostles will mark the new covenant community.
- **Victory by worship** — Jericho falls not by siege but by marching, trumpets, and a shout. The first conquest in the Promised Land is won by obedience to an absurd command. This is the logic of faith: God's methods do not need to make sense to us. They need to be obeyed.
- **Rahab** — A pagan prostitute becomes a heroine of faith (Hebrews 11:31; James 2:25) and an ancestor of Christ. The covenant promise to bless "all the families of the earth" is already at work — drawing outsiders in, crossing ethnic boundaries, demolishing the assumption that God's grace is for the worthy.
- **Hidden sin and communal consequence** — Achan's private disobedience leads to public defeat. One man's sin affects the entire community. This is the shadow side of covenant solidarity — just as one man's righteousness will one day bring justification to all (Romans 5:18).

## Christ in This Week

Joshua leads the people into the land. Jesus leads his people into the kingdom. The Jordan crossing is the Bible's second great passage through water — from death to life, from wilderness to promise — and it anticipates baptism into Christ. Rahab, saved by faith and incorporated into the covenant people, is a living demonstration of what Paul will later argue: "There is neither Jew nor Greek... for you are all one in Christ Jesus" (Galatians 3:28). And Jericho, brought down by obedient worship rather than military power, is a preview of how Christ conquers: not by the sword but by the cross.
