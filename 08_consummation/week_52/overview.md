# Week 52: From Garden to City

**Covenant:** [[overview|Consummation]]
**Week 52** | Isaiah 65–66, Ezekiel 47–48

## Overview

The final week of the study gathers the Old Testament's most luminous visions of the world to come — the new creation that every covenant has been building toward since Genesis 1. These passages do not describe repair. They describe replacement. Not a renovation of the old order but the eruption of something so radically new that the former things "shall not be remembered or come into mind" (Isaiah 65:17). And yet the new creation is not disconnected from the old. It fulfills it. The garden that opened the Bible reappears — transformed from a private paradise into a city that fills the earth, watered by a river that flows from God's throne, shaded by a tree whose leaves heal the nations.

**Isaiah 65** proclaims the vision: "Behold, I create new heavens and a new earth, and the former things shall not be remembered or come into mind. But be glad and rejoice forever in that which I create" (Isaiah 65:17-18). The new creation is not escapism — it is the world as God always intended it. Infants do not die. The elderly live in fullness. Houses are built and inhabited. Vineyards are planted and enjoyed. "The wolf and the lamb shall graze together, and the lion shall eat straw like the ox, and dust shall be the serpent's food" (Isaiah 65:25). The curse of Genesis 3 is lifted. The serpent is reduced. The world is restored.

**Isaiah 66** widens the lens to include the nations: "From new moon to new moon, and from Sabbath to Sabbath, all flesh shall come to worship before me, declares the LORD" (Isaiah 66:23). The worship that began with Abel's offering, continued through the tabernacle and temple, and was carried through exile by a faithful remnant will finally encompass all peoples.

**Ezekiel 47–48** provides the most detailed geographic vision of the restored world. A river flows from the threshold of the temple — ankle-deep, then knee-deep, then waist-deep, then a river so deep it cannot be crossed. Wherever the river flows, it brings life: "And on the banks, on both sides of the river, there will grow all kinds of trees for food. Their leaves will not wither, nor their fruit fail... Their fruit will be for food, and their leaves for healing" (Ezekiel 47:12). John will place this river in the New Jerusalem: "the river of the water of life, bright as crystal, flowing from the throne of God and of the Lamb" (Revelation 22:1). And the tree of life that was barred in Genesis 3 stands on both sides of the river, bearing fruit every month, "and the leaves of the tree were for the healing of the nations" (Revelation 22:2).

The week — and the study — closes with songs of confidence and joy. **Isaiah 25** returns for the feast where death is swallowed. **Isaiah 60** envisions a city whose light is God himself: "The sun shall be no more your light by day, nor for brightness shall the moon give you light; but the LORD will be your everlasting light, and your God will be your glory" (Isaiah 60:19). And the final readings — **Psalm 46** ("God is our refuge and strength"), **Psalm 48** ("Great is the LORD and greatly to be praised in the city of our God"), and **Isaiah 12** ("With joy you will draw water from the wells of salvation") — are the songs the redeemed will carry from this world into the next.

The Old Testament ends not in silence but in song. Not in defeat but in expectation. Not with a period but with a promise — leaning forward, straining toward the day when the one who made all things will say, "Behold, I am making all things new" (Revelation 21:5).

## Key Themes

- **New creation, not mere restoration** — Isaiah 65:17 does not describe a repaired world. It describes a new one. The distinction matters: God's final act is not maintenance. It is creation — the same creative power that spoke in Genesis 1 now producing a world where sin, death, and suffering have no foothold.
- **The river of life** — Ezekiel's temple-river is the fulfillment of Eden's river (Genesis 2:10), which watered the garden. Now it flows from God's dwelling place, deepening as it goes, bringing life to everything it touches. The river is the Spirit (John 7:38-39). The temple is Christ (John 2:21). The healing leaves are the gospel reaching the nations (Revelation 22:2).
- **No temple needed** — Ezekiel's closing words name the city: *Yahweh Shammah* — "The LORD Is There" (Ezekiel 48:35). Revelation picks up the same theme: "I saw no temple in the city, for its temple is the Lord God the Almighty and the Lamb" (Revelation 21:22). The tabernacle was a tent. The temple was a building. Christ is a body. The new creation needs no structure because God's presence fills everything.
- **The tree of life returns** — Barred since Genesis 3:24, guarded by a cherubim's flaming sword, the tree of life reappears at the end of the story — bearing twelve kinds of fruit, one for each month, its leaves "for the healing of the nations." What was lost in a garden is restored in a city. The last page of the Bible answers the first.
- **Light without sun** — Isaiah 60:19 and Revelation 21:23 both describe a city that needs no sun because God himself is its light. The light that God spoke into existence on Day 1 of creation was always pointing to this: the God who is light, dwelling with his people, illuminating everything directly, without intermediary.

## Christ in This Week

The entire study has been building toward this: the one who said "Let there be light" will himself be the light of the new creation. The one who planted a garden will build a city. The one who placed a tree of life in Eden will place it beside the river of life in the New Jerusalem. The one who walked with Adam in the cool of the day will dwell with his people forever — "and they will see his face, and his name will be on their foreheads" (Revelation 22:4). Every covenant reaches its completion in this person: the Creator who re-creates, the seed who crushes the serpent, the ark who carries the redeemed through judgment, the offspring who blesses every nation, the lamb who was slain, the king who reigns, the mediator of the better covenant, and the bridegroom who says, "Behold, I am making all things new."

The Old Testament study ends here. But the story does not. It continues in the manger, the wilderness, the upper room, the cross, the tomb, the upper room again — and one day, in a city that needs no sun, beside a river that never runs dry, under a tree whose leaves heal every wound the world has ever known.

> "Surely I am coming soon." — Revelation 22:20 (ESV)
