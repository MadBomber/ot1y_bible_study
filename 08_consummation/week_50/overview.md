# Week 50: The Day of the LORD

**Covenant:** [[overview|Consummation]]
**Week 50** | Isaiah 24–27, Joel 3, Malachi 4

## Overview

The prophets do not end with the return from exile. They end with a vision that reaches past every historical event to a day that has not yet come — a day the Old Testament calls, with terrible weight, "the day of the LORD." This is not a comfortable phrase. It is a phrase that makes the earth tremble. The prophets use it to describe the moment when God stops waiting and starts finishing — when every injustice is addressed, every evil is destroyed, every promise is kept, and the distinction between the hidden God and the visible God disappears forever.

**Isaiah 24–27** — sometimes called "Isaiah's Little Apocalypse" — paints the broadest canvas. The earth itself is devastated: "The earth is utterly broken, the earth is split apart, the earth is violently shaken" (Isaiah 24:19). The powers of heaven and earth are judged. And then, in the middle of the catastrophe, a feast: "On this mountain the LORD of hosts will make for all peoples a feast of rich food, a feast of well-aged wine" (Isaiah 25:6). And at that feast, God does the impossible: "He will swallow up death forever; and the Lord GOD will wipe away tears from all faces, and the reproach of his people he will take away from all the earth" (Isaiah 25:8). Death is not merely defeated. It is consumed. Tears are not merely dried. They are wiped away by God's own hand. Paul quotes this verse in 1 Corinthians 15:54 as the climax of his resurrection argument: "Death is swallowed up in victory." And John places it at the end of all things: "He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more" (Revelation 21:4).

**Joel 3** gathers the nations into the Valley of Decision for judgment. **Malachi 4** describes the day as a furnace — burning up the arrogant and the evildoers like stubble, but for those who fear God's name, "the sun of righteousness shall rise with healing in its wings" (Malachi 4:2). **Zephaniah 3** moves from judgment to singing: "The LORD your God is in your midst, a mighty one who will save; he will rejoice over you with gladness; he will quiet you by his love; he will exult over you with loud singing" (Zephaniah 3:17). And the psalms that close the week — 96 and 98 — envision all of creation erupting in joy: the sea roaring, the rivers clapping their hands, the hills singing together — "for he comes to judge the earth" (Psalm 98:9). In the biblical imagination, judgment is not something creation dreads. It is something creation celebrates. Because when the Judge arrives, everything wrong will finally be made right.

## Key Themes

- **Death swallowed** — Isaiah 25:8 is the Old Testament's most direct promise of death's abolition. Not managed. Not delayed. Swallowed. The image is of a predator consumed by a greater predator. Death, which has been swallowing humanity since Genesis 3, will itself be swallowed by life.
- **The feast on the mountain** — The day of the LORD is not only judgment. It is celebration. God prepares a feast for "all peoples" — not just Israel — on his holy mountain. The marriage supper of the Lamb (Revelation 19:9) is this feast in its final form.
- **Creation's joy at judgment** — Rivers clapping, hills singing, the sea roaring — the psalms portray judgment as the event creation has been waiting for. Why? Because creation has been subjected to futility by human sin (Romans 8:20). When the Judge arrives, creation is finally freed.
- **The God who sings** — Zephaniah 3:17 is the only verse in the Bible that describes God singing over his people. The one who sang the world into existence will sing over the redeemed with exultation. The story that began with God speaking ends with God singing.

## Christ in This Week

The day of the LORD is the day of Christ's return. "Death is swallowed up in victory" (1 Corinthians 15:54) — Paul places these words in the mouths of the resurrected, quoting Isaiah 25:8 as fulfilled in the resurrection of the dead. The feast Isaiah describes becomes the marriage supper of the Lamb. The sun of righteousness Malachi announces is Christ — rising with healing. And the Judge who makes the rivers clap and the hills sing is the one who was himself judged on a cross, so that his judgment of the world could be both perfectly just and perfectly merciful.
