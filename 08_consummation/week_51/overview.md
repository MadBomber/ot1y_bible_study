# Week 51: The Son of Man's Kingdom

**Covenant:** [[overview|Consummation]]
**Week 51** | Daniel 7–12, Zechariah 14

## Overview

Daniel's visions reach farther into the future than any other prophetic texts in the Old Testament. Written from exile in Babylon, they describe a sequence of empires rising and falling — each more terrible than the last — until God intervenes with a kingdom that is not of human origin, not subject to human corruption, and not limited to a single nation or era. At the center of this vision stands a figure so extraordinary that Jesus will adopt his title as his own preferred self-designation: the Son of Man.

**Daniel 7** is the chapter that reshapes Israel's messianic expectation more than any other. Four beasts emerge from the sea — a lion, a bear, a leopard, and a fourth beast "terrifying and dreadful and exceedingly strong" with iron teeth and ten horns (Daniel 7:7). These represent successive empires that dominate the earth with escalating violence. But then the scene shifts to heaven. The Ancient of Days takes his seat — his clothing white as snow, his throne flames of fire, a river of fire flowing before him. The court sits in judgment. The books are opened. And then: "I saw in the night visions, and behold, with the clouds of heaven there came one like a son of man, and he came to the Ancient of Days and was presented before him. And to him was given dominion and glory and a kingdom, that all peoples, nations, and languages should serve him; his dominion is an everlasting dominion, which shall not pass away, and his kingdom one that shall not be destroyed" (Daniel 7:13-14).

The title "Son of Man" is simultaneously human and more than human. He comes "with the clouds of heaven" — a divine prerogative. He receives universal dominion — something no mere mortal can hold. He is presented to the Ancient of Days as an equal. And his kingdom, unlike every empire the beasts represented, will never end. Jesus will use this title over eighty times in the Gospels. When the high priest asks him under oath, "Are you the Christ, the Son of the Blessed?" Jesus responds: "I am, and you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power, and coming with the clouds of heaven" (Mark 14:62). He is claiming Daniel 7. The high priest tears his robes and calls it blasphemy. He understood the claim perfectly.

**Zechariah 14** envisions the day the LORD descends to the Mount of Olives — the mountain splits in two, living waters flow from Jerusalem, and "the LORD will be king over all the earth. On that day the LORD will be one and his name one" (Zechariah 14:9). **Isaiah 11** returns to the stump of Jesse to describe a king upon whom the Spirit rests — a ruler who judges with righteousness, whose reign transforms creation itself: "The wolf shall dwell with the lamb, and the leopard shall lie down with the young goat" (Isaiah 11:6). And **Isaiah 2** lifts the eyes to the last days, when the nations stream to God's mountain and "they shall beat their swords into plowshares, and their spears into pruning hooks; nation shall not lift up sword against nation, neither shall they learn war anymore" (Isaiah 2:4).

## Key Themes

- **The Son of Man** — Daniel 7:13-14 creates the title Jesus uses most often for himself. It is both humble (emphasizing solidarity with humanity) and exalted (this figure receives universal, everlasting dominion from God). The double meaning is the point: the one who shares our nature receives the authority that only God can give.
- **Kingdoms that pass, a kingdom that remains** — The four beasts are empires — Babylon, Persia, Greece, Rome. Each seems invincible in its time. Each falls. The Son of Man's kingdom does not. The empires of the world are temporary. The kingdom of God is eternal. This is the conviction that sustained Israel in exile and sustains the church under persecution.
- **The Mount of Olives** — Zechariah places God's descent on the Mount of Olives. The disciples watch Jesus ascend from the same mountain (Acts 1:12). The angels promise he will return "in the same way" (Acts 1:11). The geography is consistent: the mountain that watched the ascension will witness the return.
- **The peaceable kingdom** — Isaiah 11's vision of predators and prey at peace is not metaphor. It is the Old Testament's picture of what creation looks like when the curse of Genesis 3 is fully reversed. The restoration is total — not just human hearts but the entire created order remade.
- **Swords into plowshares** — Isaiah 2's vision of disarmament is the ultimate political prophecy. It does not describe human achievement. It describes what happens when the righteous king reigns. War does not end because humanity wills it. War ends because the Prince of Peace rules.

## Christ in This Week

The Son of Man of Daniel 7 is Jesus' primary self-identification. He uses it to describe his authority ("The Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins" — Mark 2:10), his suffering ("The Son of Man must suffer many things" — Mark 8:31), and his return ("They will see the Son of Man coming in clouds with great power and glory" — Mark 13:26). The title holds together what no other title can: humanity and divinity, suffering and glory, humility and sovereignty. The Branch from Jesse's stump (Isaiah 11:1) is the one Paul identifies as "descended from David according to the flesh and declared to be the Son of God in power" (Romans 1:3-4). And the king who makes swords into plowshares is the one who told Peter to put away his sword (Matthew 26:52) — because the kingdom he brings conquers not by violence but by sacrifice.
