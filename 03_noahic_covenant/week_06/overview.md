# Week 6: The Flood

**Covenant:** [[overview|Noahic Covenant]]
**Week 6** | Genesis 7–8

## The Big Picture

The door shuts. God himself closes it (Genesis 7:16) — a detail so easy to miss and so impossible to forget once you see it. Noah and his family are inside. The animals are inside. And then the rain begins. For forty days and forty nights, the windows of heaven and the fountains of the great deep open simultaneously, and the world that God spoke into existence is unmade by water. The language of Genesis 7 deliberately reverses the language of Genesis 1. Where God once separated the waters above from the waters below, he now releases them. Where he once gathered the waters to let dry land appear, he now covers the land entirely. Creation is being uncreated. The flood is not merely a natural disaster — it is a theological event, a de-creation that will be followed by a re-creation, just as death will one day be followed by resurrection.

But the narrative does not dwell on destruction. Its center of gravity is a single sentence in Genesis 8:1: "But God remembered Noah." The Hebrew word *zakar* — "remembered" — does not mean God had forgotten. It means God acted on behalf of the one he had covenanted to save. It is the same word that will appear when God "remembers" his covenant with Abraham (Exodus 2:24), when Hannah prays and God "remembers" her (1 Samuel 1:19), and when the thief on the cross says, "Jesus, remember me" (Luke 23:42). To be remembered by God is to be the object of his saving attention.

The waters recede. A raven goes out. A dove goes out, returns, goes out again, and this time does not return. Noah waits — and the waiting is significant. He does not open the ark on his own authority. He waits for God's command: "Go out from the ark" (Genesis 8:16). The same obedience that built the ark now governs the exit. And Noah's first act on dry ground is worship — he builds an altar and offers burnt offerings. The aroma rises, and God responds with a promise that will sustain the world until the end of time: "I will never again curse the ground because of man... neither will I ever again strike down every living creature as I have done. While the earth remains, seedtime and harvest, cold and heat, summer and winter, day and night, shall not cease" (Genesis 8:21-22).

The week closes with Peter's remarkable theological reading of the flood in 1 Peter 3:18-22, where he draws a straight line from the waters of the flood to the waters of baptism: "Baptism, which corresponds to this, now saves you, not as a removal of dirt from the body but as an appeal to God for a good conscience, through the resurrection of Jesus Christ."

## Key Themes

- **God shuts the door** — The most terrifying detail in the narrative is also the most comforting. God himself seals the ark. Those inside are secured by divine action, not human effort. The door that saves is also the door that separates. There is an inside and an outside, and God determines which is which.
- **De-creation and re-creation** — The flood reverses Genesis 1. The ordered world returns to watery chaos. But the pattern is not destruction for its own sake — it is death followed by new life, the same pattern that will culminate in the cross and resurrection.
- **"God remembered"** — *Zakar* is one of the most important verbs in the Old Testament. When God remembers, he acts. The flood story pivots on this single word. Everything before it is judgment. Everything after it is restoration.
- **Worship before settlement** — Noah's first act on dry ground is not building a house. It is building an altar. The instinct of the redeemed is gratitude. The burnt offering that rises in Genesis 8:20 anticipates the entire sacrificial system — and the one final sacrifice that will make all others unnecessary.

## Christ in This Week

The ark carried eight people through the waters of judgment into a new world. Peter sees in this a picture of baptism — and behind baptism, the resurrection of Jesus Christ (1 Peter 3:21). The flood is death. The ark is Christ. The new world on the other side is resurrection life. The God who "shut him in" is the same God who sealed the tomb and then opened it. And the promise God makes after the flood — "never again" — anticipates the finality of the cross: a judgment so complete that it need never be repeated.
