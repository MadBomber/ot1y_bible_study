# Week 5: Corruption and Grace

**Covenant:** [[overview|Noahic Covenant]]
**Week 5** | Genesis 6

## The Big Picture

The genealogy of Genesis 5 ended with a whisper of hope — Noah, whose name echoes the Hebrew word for rest. But the world Noah inherits is anything but restful. Genesis 6 opens with one of the most disturbing assessments in all of Scripture: "The LORD saw that the wickedness of man was great in the earth, and that every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually" (Genesis 6:5). Not some intentions. Not most. Every intention. Continually. The corruption is total. And God's response is not indifference — it is grief: "And the LORD regretted that he had made man on the earth, and it grieved him to his heart" (Genesis 6:6). This is not the language of a distant, unfeeling deity. This is the language of a Father watching his children destroy themselves.

But then comes the most important conjunction in the chapter: "But." "But Noah found favor in the eyes of the LORD" (Genesis 6:8). The Hebrew word is *chen* — grace. It appears here for the first time in Scripture, and its placement is deliberate. Before Noah does anything righteous, before he builds a single plank of the ark, grace finds him. He is described in the next verse as "a righteous man, blameless in his generation" who "walked with God" — but the text is careful to put grace before righteousness, not after it. Noah's obedience flows from grace, not the other way around.

What follows is God's detailed blueprint for salvation: an ark of gopher wood, 300 cubits long, sealed with pitch, with one door. The specificity matters. God does not tell Noah to figure it out. He provides the design. The ark is not Noah's idea — it is God's provision, constructed according to God's instructions, for a judgment that God will send and a salvation that God will accomplish. Noah's role is to believe and obey. Everything else is God's.

The week closes with two NT perspectives on this moment. Peter reads the flood as a typological pattern for the final judgment, noting that God "did not spare the ancient world, but preserved Noah, a herald of righteousness" (2 Peter 2:5). And Ezekiel, writing centuries later during the Babylonian exile, uses Noah (alongside Daniel and Job) as an example of individual righteousness that cannot save a corrupt nation — a sobering reminder that God's patience has limits even as his grace has no bottom.

## Key Themes

- **Total corruption** — Genesis 6:5 is the Bible's darkest diagnosis of the human condition. The fall of Genesis 3 has metastasized across ten generations into something universal and unrelenting. This is not a bad neighborhood; it is a terminal diagnosis for the entire species.
- **Divine grief** — God is not detached from his creation's ruin. He is grieved "to his heart." The God of the Bible is not impassible in the philosophical sense — he feels the weight of what his creatures have become. This grief will surface again at Gethsemane, where the Son of Man will be "sorrowful, even to death" (Matthew 26:38).
- **Grace before obedience** — *Chen* precedes description of Noah's righteousness. This is the Bible's first statement of a principle Paul will later make explicit: "By grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God" (Ephesians 2:8).
- **God designs the salvation** — The ark's dimensions, materials, and structure come from God, not Noah. Salvation is not a human innovation. It is a divine provision, offered before the judgment arrives, entered through one door.

## Christ in This Week

The ark is the Bible's first extended picture of Christ as the means of salvation. God provides it. God designs it. It has one door — and those who enter through it are saved from the judgment that destroys everything outside. Jesus will later say, "I am the door. If anyone enters by me, he will be saved" (John 10:9). The God who grieves over a corrupt world in Genesis 6 is the same God who will weep over Jerusalem (Luke 19:41) and sweat drops of blood in a garden (Luke 22:44). And the grace that finds Noah before he does anything to deserve it is the same grace that finds every sinner who has ever called on the name of the Lord.
