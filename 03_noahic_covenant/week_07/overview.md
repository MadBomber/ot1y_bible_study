# Week 7: A New Beginning

**Covenant:** [[overview|Noahic Covenant]]
**Week 7** | Genesis 9

## The Big Picture

Noah steps out of the ark into a world that looks like Genesis 1 all over again. The parallels are deliberate. God blesses Noah and his sons with the same words he spoke over Adam: "Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth" (Genesis 9:1; cf. Genesis 1:28). Humanity is given a fresh start — a second creation, a new beginning. But this is not Eden restored. The differences are as significant as the similarities. Animals will now fear humans (Genesis 9:2). Humans may now eat meat — but not with the blood, "for the life of the flesh is in the blood" (cf. Leviticus 17:11). And for the first time, God institutes a penalty for murder: "Whoever sheds the blood of man, by man shall his blood be shed, for God made man in his own image" (Genesis 9:6). The image of God still marks every human being — but the world in which that image exists is now a world that requires restraint, accountability, and the sober recognition that violence is not merely a social problem but an assault on the divine likeness.

Then comes the covenant — the first time Scripture uses the term *berith* in the context of a formal divine commitment. God binds himself not only to Noah and his descendants but to "every living creature" and to "the earth" itself (Genesis 9:10, 13). The scope is staggering. This is not a tribal agreement. It is a cosmic one. And its sign is the rainbow — in Hebrew, the same word (*qesheth*) used for a warrior's bow. God hangs his weapon in the sky, pointed away from the earth. The sign of the covenant is a disarmed God — or rather, a God who has turned his judgment away from his creation and committed to patience. Peter will later explain why: "The Lord is not slow to fulfill his promise as some count slowness, but is patient toward you, not wishing that any should perish, but that all should reach repentance" (2 Peter 3:9).

But the new beginning is immediately complicated. Noah plants a vineyard, drinks himself into a stupor, and lies naked in his tent. His son Ham "saw the nakedness of his father" — a phrase that in the ancient world carried connotations of deep dishonor — and told his brothers. Shem and Japheth walk in backward, covering their father without looking. The episode is brief and strange, but its point is sharp: the flood destroyed the old world, but it did not destroy the old nature. Sin survived the ark. The problem of Genesis 3 has not been solved by Genesis 9. Something more than a flood will be needed.

The week closes with two passages that draw out the covenant's long trajectory. Isaiah 54:9-10 uses the Noahic covenant as a metaphor for God's steadfast love: "As I swore that the waters of Noah should no more go over the earth, so I have sworn that I will not be angry with you." And 2 Peter 3:3-13 reveals that the flood was a pattern — a rehearsal for a final judgment that will come not by water but by fire, and that will give way not to a cleaned-up version of the old world but to "new heavens and a new earth in which righteousness dwells."

## Key Themes

- **New creation, old nature** — Genesis 9 echoes Genesis 1, but the differences reveal that the flood is a reset, not a solution. The human heart that produced Genesis 6:5 has not been changed. Noah's drunkenness and Ham's dishonor demonstrate that the sin problem requires something the flood cannot provide: internal transformation. The new covenant of Jeremiah 31 and Ezekiel 36 is already being anticipated by what Genesis 9 leaves unresolved.
- **The sanctity of life and blood** — The prohibition against consuming blood and the institution of capital punishment both rest on the same foundation: human beings are made in God's image, and their blood is sacred. This principle will undergird the entire sacrificial system and will find its ultimate expression in the blood of Christ, "the image of the invisible God" (Colossians 1:15), shed for the life of the world.
- **Covenant with all creation** — The Noahic covenant is unique in its scope. It includes animals, the earth, and every future generation. God binds himself to sustain the entire created order. This is the theological foundation for what theologians call "common grace" — God's ongoing provision for all creatures regardless of their response to him.
- **The warrior's bow disarmed** — The rainbow is not a decoration. It is a weapon laid down. God commits to patience — not because the world deserves it, but because his redemptive plan requires time. Every sunrise and every harvest is a gift purchased by this covenant (Genesis 8:22).

## Christ in This Week

The new beginning after the flood points forward to the ultimate new beginning after the resurrection. Noah steps into a washed world; Christ rises into a conquered death. The commission "Be fruitful and multiply" will find its Great Commission counterpart: "Go therefore and make disciples of all nations" (Matthew 28:19). The blood that God declares sacred in Genesis 9:6 is the same blood that Christ will pour out as "the new covenant in my blood" (Luke 22:20). And the rainbow — God's sign that judgment has passed and patience reigns — finds its final echo in Revelation 4:3, where a rainbow encircles the throne of God. Even in the throne room of final judgment, mercy frames the picture.
